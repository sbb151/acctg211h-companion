12  Performance Measurement as Agency

12.1 Opening question

In the late 1990s and through the 2000s, Wells Fargo built an internal incentive system that rewarded retail employees for opening new accounts for existing customers — a practice the bank called cross-selling (i.e., persuading a checking-account customer to also take a savings account, a credit card, and so on). The system became famous internally as Eight is Great: the stated aspiration was that the average retail customer should hold eight separate Wells Fargo accounts. Branch employees were assigned daily and weekly cross-sell quotas; their compensation, performance evaluations, and continued employment were tied to the quotas; and the bank cited cross-sell metrics as a competitive advantage in its investor disclosures.

The system worked, in the narrow sense that cross-sell metrics rose. It also produced one of the largest banking scandals of the 2010s. Wells Fargo employees had opened millions of accounts that customers had not authorized — in some cases by forging signatures, in others by registering customers for products they had never requested. When the practice became public in 2016, the bank paid roughly $185 million in regulatory penalties, fired thousands of employees, and saw its CEO resign under pressure from the board and from members of Congress. Subsequent litigation and supervisory action cost the bank several billion dollars and substantially damaged its franchise.

The Wells Fargo case is the canonical illustration of a problem that the academic literature on performance measurement has been developing for decades. Every performance metric a firm uses is an imperfect measure of what the firm actually wants the employee to do. The metric captures some dimensions of the desired behavior but misses others; it captures observable activity but not the underlying judgment, effort, or care that the activity is meant to reflect. The employee, responding rationally to the incentives the metric creates, optimizes against the metric — including in dimensions the firm did not intend and that may damage the firm’s interests. The literature on performance measurement is, in important respects, the analytical study of this distortion.

This chapter develops the framework. The framework is, in its formal structure, a continuation of the moral hazard analysis of Chapter 4 — the agent has private information about her actions, the principal observes only a noisy signal of those actions, and the principal designs a contract that conditions on the available signals. The added complication is that the agent’s actions are multidimensional: the agent allocates effort across multiple tasks, only some of which the principal can directly contract on. The classic statement is Holmstrom and Milgrom (1991), “Multitask Principal-Agent Analyses,” who showed that strong incentives on the measurable tasks predictably crowd out effort on the unmeasurable ones. The Wells Fargo case is that result in its most spectacular form.

The chapter introduces the standard internal performance metrics that firms use — return on investment (ROI), residual income (RI), economic value added (EVA), and the balanced scorecard — and develops each as a particular response to the multitasking problem. None of these metrics is a clean solution. Each has characteristic distortions of its own. The framework gives us a tool for predicting which distortions a given metric will produce, and the chapter’s discussion questions and problems ask the student to apply the tool to specific design problems.

12.2 The multitasking framework

Consider an extension of the LEN model from Chapter 4 in which the agent allocates effort across multiple tasks. Let the agent’s effort vector be \(\mathbf{e} = (e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_n)\), with each component \(e_i\) representing effort on task \(i\). The cost of effort is \(C(\mathbf{e})\), an increasing convex function with substitution: time spent on task 1 is time not spent on tasks 2 through \(n\). The firm’s true preferences are a weighted combination of the tasks, with weights \(b_1, \ldots, b_n\) that reflect what the firm actually values.

The contracting problem arises because the principal cannot directly observe \(\mathbf{e}\). The principal observes only signals of the form \(\mathbf{y}_i = e_i + \varepsilon_i\) for some subset of the tasks \(i \in S\), where \(S\) is the set of contractible tasks. For tasks \(i \notin S\), the principal cannot construct a contract that directly rewards effort — she may know the agent is doing the work in some general sense, but no measurable signal can be tied to compensation.

The principal designs a linear contract on the observable signals, \(w(\mathbf{y}) = a + \sum_{i \in S} \alpha_i y_i\). The agent then allocates effort to maximize her own certainty-equivalent payoff. Holmstrom and Milgrom show that the agent responds in a specific way: she supplies more effort to tasks with high \(\alpha_i\) and less effort to the lightly-weighted tasks — including, critically, the tasks not contracted on at all because they are not observable.

The central result is that the optimal contract weights \(\alpha_i\) are not simply the firm’s underlying preferences \(b_i\). The optimal weights account for the substitution patterns the agent will exhibit across tasks: if a high \(\alpha_i\) on task 1 will cause the agent to underprovide effort on tasks 2 through \(n\), the optimal contract may set \(\alpha_1\) below \(b_1\) to limit the distortion. In the extreme case where the unobservable tasks are sufficiently important, the optimal contract may set \(\alpha_i = 0\) on the observable tasks — a flat wage — because incentivizing the observable tasks would produce a worse distortion in the unobservable tasks than the gain from incentivizing the observable tasks.

The central insight

The Holmstrom-Milgrom result is often stated more simply: incentivizing what you can measure crowds out what you cannot measure. The Wells Fargo case is the canonical illustration. The bank could measure cross-sell counts cheaply and accurately; it could not, at low cost, measure customer-service quality, account integrity, or the absence of fraud. Incentivizing the measurable dimension produced a substitution away from the unmeasurable dimensions, with consequences that ultimately swamped the gains from the measured behavior.

The multitasking framework has implications well beyond the Wells Fargo case. It predicts that any performance-measurement system using a narrow set of metrics will produce predictable distortions in the dimensions the metrics do not capture. (i) A teacher whose pay depends on test scores may neglect dimensions of student development the tests do not measure. (ii) An emergency-room physician evaluated on average length-of-stay may discharge patients prematurely. (iii) A salesperson whose commission is tied to closed deals may neglect customer relationships, follow-through, and account quality. The pattern is consistent across organizational settings, and the framework explains why. We will return to the empirical evidence in Section 12.7.

12.3 Return on investment

The most widely-used internal performance metric in U.S. firms is return on investment (ROI) — operating profit divided by the assets deployed in the unit being measured:

\[ \text{ROI} = \frac{\text{Operating profit}}{\text{Average operating assets}}. \]

The metric is intuitive: it reports the profit the unit produces per dollar of assets it controls. It is also comparable across units of different sizes, since the denominator scales with the unit’s asset base. A division earning $50 million on $250 million of assets has an ROI of 20 percent; a division earning $200 million on $2 billion of assets has an ROI of 10 percent. ROI is widely used at investment-center boundaries (i.e., where the manager controls capital deployment) and in cross-divisional comparisons within multidivisional firms.

The ROI metric has, however, a well-known distortion. A manager whose performance is evaluated on ROI has an incentive to underinvest — specifically, to refuse projects whose ROI is below the unit’s current ROI but above the firm’s cost of capital. The mechanism is straightforward. A division currently earning 20 percent ROI is presented with a new investment opportunity that would earn 15 percent. The 15 percent return exceeds the firm’s cost of capital (assume, say, 10 percent), so the project creates value for the firm. But accepting the project would reduce the division’s average ROI from 20 percent toward 15 percent, which would harm the divisional manager’s measured performance. The manager’s rational response is to decline the project, even though the firm’s interests favor accepting it.

The underinvestment distortion is the canonical problem of ROI as a performance metric. It is large enough in some settings that firms have moved to alternative metrics — particularly residual income and economic value added — which we develop next.

12.4 Residual income, EVA, and a worked comparison

Residual income (RI) addresses the ROI underinvestment distortion by measuring the dollar amount of profit the unit produces in excess of a capital charge. The capital charge is the firm’s required return times the assets deployed in the unit:

\[ \text{Residual income} = \text{Operating profit} - r \times \text{Average operating assets}, \]

where \(r\) is the firm’s required rate of return on the capital deployed.

Residual income has the property that any project whose return exceeds the cost of capital increases the unit’s residual income. The 15 percent project from Section 12.3 would, under residual income, increase the unit’s measured performance: the project earns more than the cost of capital, so it produces positive residual income. The underinvestment distortion is eliminated.

Economic value added (EVA), a trademark of the consulting firm Stern Stewart, is a refinement of residual income developed in the 1990s and adopted by a substantial number of large U.S. firms during that period. EVA differs from residual income in two specific respects. First, EVA adjusts the operating profit and asset base for items the firm believes are not informative about underlying performance — typically, (i) R&D is capitalized and amortized rather than expensed, (ii) operating leases are capitalized, (iii) goodwill amortization is added back, and (iv) deferred-tax adjustments are unwound. Second, EVA uses the firm’s weighted-average cost of capital (WACC) — a blended return reflecting both equity and debt financing — rather than the cost of equity alone. The result is a metric that is, in principle, a closer approximation to the unit’s economic value creation than residual income computed from unadjusted GAAP numbers.

A worked comparison

Consider the Acme Imaging Division, an investment center within Acme Corp. The division’s reported numbers for the most recent year are: operating profit of $40 million, average operating assets of $200 million, R&D expense of $15 million (treated by GAAP as a period cost), and an operating-lease commitment with a capitalized value of $30 million (off-balance-sheet under historical GAAP). Acme’s cost of equity is 12 percent and its weighted-average cost of capital is 9 percent. The numbers are stylized but representative of a mid-sized industrial division.

ROI. The division’s ROI is $40 / \(200 = 20.0\) percent.

Residual income. Using the cost of equity as the capital charge, residual income equals $40 - 0.12 = 40 - 24 = $16 million.

EVA. The EVA computation adjusts the operating profit and the asset base. To capitalize R&D, we add back the current-year R&D expense to operating profit and reduce it by the amortization of the capitalized R&D balance; assume the net effect on operating profit is $+$10 million (i.e., R&D capitalization produces a $10 million increase in adjusted operating profit). To capitalize the operating leases, we add the $30 million capitalized lease value to the asset base. The adjusted operating profit is $40 + 10 = $50 million; the adjusted asset base is $200 + 15 + 30 = $245 million, where the $15 million reflects the net capitalized R&D asset (current-year expense capitalized, less the prior amortization). EVA equals $50 - 0.09 = 50 - 22.05 = $27.95 million.

The three metrics paint very different pictures of the same division. ROI reports a clean 20 percent return. Residual income reports $16 million of value creation above the equity-financing benchmark. EVA reports $27.95 million of value creation above the WACC benchmark, after correcting for the GAAP treatments Stern Stewart considers misleading. The metric a firm chooses is, in important respects, a choice about which set of distortions the firm is willing to tolerate.

Now consider the same division evaluating a new investment opportunity: a $50 million capital expenditure that would produce $7 million of incremental operating profit, with no associated R&D or leasing complications. The project’s standalone ROI is $7 / \(50 = 14.0\) percent. The project’s residual income contribution is $7 - 0.12 = 7 - 6 = +$1 million; the project’s EVA contribution is $7 - 0.09 = 7 - 4.5 = +$2.5 million.

A manager evaluated on ROI rejects the project: the project’s 14 percent ROI is below the division’s existing 20 percent ROI, and accepting the project would dilute the divisional average. A manager evaluated on residual income accepts the project: the $+$1 million RI contribution is positive. A manager evaluated on EVA accepts the project a fortiori: the $+$2.5 million EVA contribution is positive and larger than the RI contribution because the capital charge is lower under WACC than under the cost of equity.

The example makes concrete the broad claim of Sections 12.3 and 12.4: ROI produces a predictable underinvestment distortion that the dollar-based metrics correct, and the choice between residual income and EVA depends on how much of the GAAP-driven measurement noise the firm believes Stern Stewart-style adjustments actually correct.

Common pitfall

Residual income and EVA are sometimes presented as superior to ROI on the grounds that they “are correct” while ROI “is incorrect.” The framing is too strong. Residual income and EVA correct the underinvestment distortion of ROI, but they introduce other distortions of their own. (i) A manager evaluated on residual income may overstate the unit’s contribution to firm value by neglecting cross-divisional spillovers. (ii) A manager may resist asset sales that would reduce the capital charge even when the asset is genuinely impaired. (iii) The dollar-based metrics make small divisions look small in absolute terms, which can complicate compensation comparisons across units of different sizes. Each metric is the result of design choices, and each metric produces characteristic distortions. The right comparison is among the distortions, not between “right” and “wrong” metrics.

12.5 The balanced scorecard

The balanced scorecard, developed by Kaplan and Norton (1992), is a non-quantitative response to the multitasking problem. Rather than pick a single metric and live with its distortions, the firm uses a portfolio of performance metrics covering multiple dimensions of the unit’s activity. The original Kaplan-Norton framework identifies four perspectives: (i) financial (the traditional accounting metrics — ROI, residual income, EVA, growth in operating profit); (ii) customer (customer satisfaction, market share, retention, net promoter score); (iii) internal business processes (cycle times, defect rates, time-to-market for new products); and (iv) learning and growth (employee skill development, training hours, organizational capacity, employee retention).

The balanced-scorecard approach has been widely adopted in U.S. firms over the past three decades. The motivation, in the framework of Section 12.2, is that a portfolio of metrics covers more of the dimensions of the agent’s effort than any single metric could; the multitasking distortions of any single metric are partially mitigated by the inclusion of other metrics capturing the dimensions the first metric missed. If a sales manager is evaluated on revenue alone, she has an incentive to maximize revenue at the expense of customer satisfaction; if she is evaluated on revenue and on a customer-satisfaction index, the substitution toward revenue-only behavior is dampened.

The balanced-scorecard approach has limitations of its own. (i) Adding metrics is easy; weighting them appropriately is hard. The literature has documented that, in practice, firms that adopt balanced-scorecard frameworks often revert to a small number of dominant metrics — typically the financial ones — when compensation decisions are made. The remaining metrics influence compensation only at the margin, if at all. (ii) Non-financial metrics are often constructed inside the firm, which means the manager has greater influence over the measurement process than for audited financial numbers. A customer-satisfaction score built from a survey the manager designs is not the same kind of signal as a residual-income number built from audited financial statements. (iii) Multiple metrics also create multiple gaming opportunities: a manager evaluated on five dimensions may game all five, more cheaply per dimension than she could game a single dimension that received heavier scrutiny.

Ittner and Larcker (2003) reviewed the empirical evidence and concluded that the implementation of balanced-scorecard systems is harder than the conceptual framework suggests, and that many adopting firms do not realize the full benefits the framework predicts. Their finding is consistent with the broader pattern in this chapter: the balanced scorecard is a partial response to the multitasking problem, not a clean solution. The trade-off between simplicity and completeness in performance measurement does not have an obvious resolution; the right balance varies by firm, by industry, and by the specific roles being measured.

12.6 Variance analysis

The chapter has so far focused on metrics that compare actual performance to a benchmark — the cost of capital, the firm’s overall preferences, a portfolio of dimensions. A separate but related set of tools compares actual performance to a budget or standard, decomposing the difference into component pieces that identify where the deviation came from. This is the domain of variance analysis.

A variance is the difference between an actual value and a planned (i.e., budgeted or standard) value for a particular cost or revenue item. The standard decomposition produces three components. (i) A price variance captures the portion of the difference attributable to a difference between actual and standard prices. (ii) A quantity variance captures the portion attributable to a difference between actual and standard quantities consumed or produced. (iii) A mix variance captures the portion attributable to differences in the mix of products or inputs. The decomposition points the analyst toward the cause of the deviation: a cost overrun dominated by price variances suggests pursuing supplier renegotiations; one dominated by quantity variances suggests pursuing operational improvements; one dominated by mix variances suggests pursuing product-line review.

Variance analysis is a workhorse of internal management accounting. It is widely taught in introductory managerial accounting courses, and the mechanics are largely standardized across textbooks. We will not work through the formulas in detail here; the standard treatment is available in any intermediate textbook, and the problems for this chapter ask you to apply variance analysis to specific examples.

The framework of this chapter qualifies the value of variance analysis in a way the standard treatments often miss. A variance is a deviation from a benchmark, but the benchmark itself is a design choice. A standard cost set too tight produces unfavorable variances even when the firm is performing well; a standard cost set too loose produces favorable variances even when the firm is underperforming. The standard-setting process is itself subject to the multitasking distortions: a manager who negotiates her own standards has an incentive to negotiate loose ones, producing favorable variances at the cost of disguising underperformance. Variance analysis is most useful when the standards are credible — set by an outside party, indexed to external benchmarks, or otherwise made resistant to the manager’s negotiating influence.

12.7 Empirical evidence

The empirical literature on performance measurement is large; Ittner and Larcker (2003) and the subsequent literature on management control systems provide useful entry points. Four findings are particularly relevant to the framework of this chapter.

First, the consequences of multitasking distortions are real and measurable in settings far beyond the Wells Fargo case. In health care, the introduction of publicly-reported surgical-mortality scorecards for cardiac surgeons in New York and Pennsylvania in the early 1990s was followed by a pronounced pattern of patient selection: surgeons whose mortality rates were being scored became more selective about which patients they would operate on, declining the sickest patients whose deaths would worsen the surgeon’s reported numbers. The sickest patients were redirected to out-of-state hospitals or denied surgery altogether. The reported mortality rates improved, but at the cost of access for the patients most in need of care — a substitution toward the measurable dimension (reported mortality) and away from the unmeasurable dimension (treating the sickest patients). The pattern is consistent with the Holmstrom-Milgrom framework and has been documented across several empirical studies in the health-economics literature.

Second, the school-accountability literature documents a parallel pattern in education. The introduction of high-stakes standardized testing under No Child Left Behind (2001) and the state-level accountability systems that preceded it produced clear evidence of “teaching to the test” — substitution of instructional time toward tested subjects (reading and math) and away from untested subjects (science, history, art), substitution toward the specific question formats appearing on the tests, and, in extreme cases, outright cheating by teachers and administrators. The Chicago Public Schools test-tampering scandal of the early 2000s, documented at length in the economics literature, was the most striking case: statistical analysis of answer patterns revealed that a substantial fraction of teachers were systematically altering student answer sheets to inflate scores. The pattern of “what gets measured gets managed” — including its darker manifestations — is the multitasking framework in operation.

Third, sales-quota gaming is a recurring pattern in the corporate-incentive literature. The standard finding is that sales force compensation tied to quarterly quotas produces end-of-quarter pull-forwards (i.e., sales artificially advanced from the next quarter into the current quarter to clear the quota) and beginning-of-quarter pushbacks (i.e., sales artificially delayed when the current quarter is unsalvageable, to give the next quarter a head start). The pattern is observable in firm-level revenue time series and has been used in academic studies to identify the incidence of quota-based compensation in specific industries. The case study most often used in classroom settings is the Sears Auto Centers episode of 1992, in which mechanics evaluated on per-hour repair revenue were found to be recommending unnecessary repairs — substitution toward the measurable dimension (revenue per labor hour) and away from the unmeasurable dimension (only recommending repairs the customer actually needed).

Fourth, the substitution from short-term to long-term metrics in U.S. compensation contracts has been documented in recent decades. Many firms have moved from purely annual performance metrics toward mixes that include multi-year performance shares, relative-performance evaluation, and clawback provisions tied to long-term outcomes. The substitution is consistent with the prediction that the multitasking distortions of short-term metrics can be partially mitigated by introducing longer-horizon evaluations, though the empirical evidence on whether the change has actually reduced gaming is mixed.

The four findings together support the broad claim of this chapter: performance metrics produce real behavioral responses, and the responses include both the desired changes (more effort on the measured dimension) and the predictable distortions (less effort on the unmeasured dimensions). The framework of Section 12.2 is, by the standards of empirical economics, an unusually well-supported piece of theory.

A case worth knowing: GE under Welch

General Electric under Jack Welch (CEO from 1981 to 2001) is the most-studied corporate case of single-metric-driven management. Welch ran GE on a small set of dominant financial metrics — operating margin, return on equity, and “Six Sigma” defect rates — and tied substantial divisional compensation to those metrics. The strategy produced two decades of extraordinary reported performance and made Welch one of the most-admired executives of his generation. It also produced, in retrospect, a set of distortions consistent with the multitasking framework. GE Capital grew into a financial-services giant in part because reported earnings from financial-services operations could be smoothed across periods in ways that operating-business earnings could not — the unit became, in important respects, an instrument for managing the firm’s reported metrics. Several of GE’s industrial businesses underinvested in research and capital expenditure during the Welch era, producing strong short-term margins at the cost of competitive position in the 2000s and 2010s. The post-Welch decline of GE is, in the framework of this chapter, exactly the pattern the multitasking theory predicts: extraordinary measured performance during the incentive regime, followed by the slow revelation of the dimensions that were not being measured.

12.8 Discussion questions

  1. The Wells Fargo case is presented as the canonical illustration of the multitasking problem. Identify another widely-discussed case — in any organizational setting (i.e., corporate, educational, governmental, athletic) — in which a measurable performance metric induced substitution away from unmeasurable dimensions of the desired behavior. Use the framework of Section 12.2 to explain the mechanism.
  2. The chapter argues that ROI produces an underinvestment distortion that residual income corrects. Yet ROI remains widely used in U.S. firms, including in firms whose CFOs are sophisticated enough to understand the distortion. Why might a firm prefer ROI to residual income despite the known distortion?
  3. The Holmstrom-Milgrom result implies that, in the extreme, the optimal contract on the observable tasks may be a flat wage. Identify a real-world setting in which something close to a flat wage is the dominant compensation arrangement, in a way that the multitasking framework would predict.
  4. Variance analysis decomposes deviations from budget into price, quantity, and mix components. Suppose a firm’s operations group consistently reports favorable price variances and unfavorable quantity variances. What does the pattern suggest about the firm’s standard-setting process and its operational performance? Be specific.
  5. The balanced scorecard adds non-financial metrics to address the limitations of financial-metric-only systems. The empirical evidence is mixed. Suppose you were advising a firm on whether to adopt a balanced-scorecard approach. What conditions would lead you to recommend adoption, and what conditions would lead you to recommend against it?
Key takeaways
  • Every performance metric is an imperfect signal. A metric captures some dimensions of desired behavior and misses others; the missed dimensions are where the distortion lives.
  • The multitasking result is the deepest theorem in incentive theory. When effort is multidimensional and only some tasks are measurable, strong incentives on the measurable tasks predictably crowd out effort on the unmeasurable ones (Holmstrom and Milgrom 1991).
  • ROI produces underinvestment. A manager evaluated on ROI rejects positive-NPV projects that would dilute the divisional ROI average, even when those projects create value for the firm.
  • Residual income and EVA correct underinvestment but introduce their own distortions. The choice among ROI, RI, and EVA is a choice among distortion profiles, not a choice between right and wrong metrics.
  • The balanced scorecard is a partial response, not a solution. Adding non-financial metrics dampens the multitasking distortion of any single metric but adds weighting and gaming problems of its own (Kaplan and Norton 1992; Ittner and Larcker 2003).
  • What gets measured gets managed — for good and for ill. Hospital scorecards, school accountability, and sales-quota gaming all show the same pattern: measurable dimensions improve, unmeasurable dimensions deteriorate, and the net welfare effect depends on the relative magnitudes.

12.9 Problems

  1. (Numerical, ROI vs. residual income.) A division of Acme Corp. currently earns operating profit of $40 million on average operating assets of $200 million. The firm’s cost of capital is 10 percent. (a) Compute the division’s ROI and residual income. (b) Suppose the divisional manager is presented with a new project requiring $50 million of investment that will produce $7 million of incremental operating profit. Compute the project’s standalone ROI, the division’s post-project ROI, and the project’s contribution to residual income. (c) Will a manager evaluated on ROI accept the project? Will a manager evaluated on residual income? Explain.
  2. (Numerical, EVA.) Continuing Problem 1, suppose Acme is considering moving from residual income to EVA. The relevant adjustments increase the divisional asset base by $20 million (i.e., capitalizing R&D that GAAP expenses) and increase operating profit by $3 million (i.e., reducing the corresponding R&D expense recognized in the period). Acme’s weighted-average cost of capital is 9 percent — lower than the equity-only 10 percent because of debt financing. (a) Compute the division’s EVA. (b) Compare the EVA result to the residual-income result from Problem 1 and explain, in one or two sentences, why the EVA figure is larger.
  3. (Numerical, marginal-project decision.) The Bravo Division of Acme currently earns ROI of 18 percent on an asset base of $500 million; its cost of equity is 11 percent and its WACC is 8 percent. Two new projects are under consideration. Project A requires $100 million of investment and produces $13 million of incremental operating profit; Project B requires $40 million and produces $4 million. (a) Compute the standalone ROI, the residual-income contribution (at the cost of equity), and the EVA contribution (at WACC) for each project. (b) Identify which projects a manager evaluated on each metric would accept. (c) Identify any project for which the three metrics disagree, and explain the source of the disagreement.
  4. (Numerical, balanced scorecard.) The Charlie Division of Acme is evaluated on four metrics under a balanced-scorecard system: operating margin (weight 40 percent), customer-satisfaction index (weight 25 percent), on-time delivery rate (weight 20 percent), and employee retention rate (weight 15 percent). The divisional manager faces a fixed time budget across the four dimensions and chooses effort to maximize her weighted bonus. (a) If the manager’s marginal product of effort is roughly equal across the four dimensions, what is her predicted effort allocation under the scorecard? (b) Suppose the firm wants to shift the manager’s effort more heavily toward customer satisfaction. The CEO is considering two options: increasing the customer-satisfaction weight to 40 percent and reducing operating margin to 25 percent; or holding the weights fixed but introducing a “knockout” provision under which a customer-satisfaction score below 80 percent eliminates the bonus entirely. Compare the predicted behavioral effects of the two options, drawing on the framework of Section 12.2.
  5. (Conceptual, multitask design.) You are designing a performance-evaluation system for the managers of a national chain of urgent-care medical clinics. The firm cares about three dimensions of clinic performance: (i) patient throughput (i.e., number of patients seen per day), (ii) clinical quality (i.e., the accuracy of diagnoses and appropriateness of treatments), and (iii) patient satisfaction. Throughput is easily measured; clinical quality is measurable in principle (through chart audits) but at substantial cost; patient satisfaction is measurable through surveys, with all the limitations surveys carry. In approximately 400 words, propose a performance-measurement system for the clinic managers. Be specific about which dimensions you would incentivize, with what weights, and how you would mitigate the multitasking distortions you anticipate.
  6. (Conceptual, real-world gaming.) Choose a specific real-world episode in which a performance metric appears to have induced gaming or other distortion. Candidate examples include the Wells Fargo cross-selling scandal (banking), the Chicago Public Schools test-tampering scandal (education), surgical-mortality reporting and patient selection (health care), the Sears Auto Centers commission scandal (retail), or any other episode the student can document from public sources. In approximately 350 words, (a) describe the metric and the incentive structure, (b) identify the substitution the framework of Section 12.2 predicts, (c) describe the actual behavioral response that occurred, and (d) propose a change to the incentive structure that would have reduced the distortion.
  7. (Conceptual, reading.) Read Holmstrom and Milgrom (1991), focusing on the introduction and the simplest version of the multitasking model. In approximately 350 words, summarize the central result and explain how it applies to the Wells Fargo case. Identify one feature of the Wells Fargo incentive structure that, if it had been changed, would have reduced the distortion.
  8. (Writing exercise.) Choose a specific role within an organization you know well (i.e., a job you or a family member has held, a position at a campus organization, a role at a firm you have studied). In approximately 500 words, write a memo to the person who designs the performance-evaluation system for that role. The memo should (i) identify three dimensions of effort the role requires, (ii) describe the metrics currently in use and the distortions they produce, and (iii) propose a revised metric portfolio. Adopt a measured, third-person professional tone throughout — the memo is a deliverable, not an internal monologue.

12.10 Lab

The in-class activity for the second meeting of this week is a performance-evaluation simulation building on the existing performance evaluation 2.xlsx materials. You will be assigned a role as either a divisional manager or a corporate evaluator and will play out a sequence of compensation-design and effort-allocation decisions. The simulation makes the multitasking framework concrete by showing, in real time, how a particular performance metric induces predictable distortions in the manager’s behavior.

# Performance Measurement as Agency {#sec-performance}

## Opening question

In the late 1990s and through the 2000s, Wells Fargo built an internal incentive system that rewarded retail employees for opening new accounts for existing customers — a practice the bank called *cross-selling* (i.e., persuading a checking-account customer to also take a savings account, a credit card, and so on). The system became famous internally as *Eight is Great*: the stated aspiration was that the average retail customer should hold eight separate Wells Fargo accounts. Branch employees were assigned daily and weekly cross-sell quotas; their compensation, performance evaluations, and continued employment were tied to the quotas; and the bank cited cross-sell metrics as a competitive advantage in its investor disclosures.

The system worked, in the narrow sense that cross-sell metrics rose. It also produced one of the largest banking scandals of the 2010s. Wells Fargo employees had opened millions of accounts that customers had not authorized — in some cases by forging signatures, in others by registering customers for products they had never requested. When the practice became public in 2016, the bank paid roughly $185 million in regulatory penalties, fired thousands of employees, and saw its CEO resign under pressure from the board and from members of Congress. Subsequent litigation and supervisory action cost the bank several billion dollars and substantially damaged its franchise.

The Wells Fargo case is the canonical illustration of a problem that the academic literature on performance measurement has been developing for decades. Every performance metric a firm uses is an imperfect measure of what the firm actually wants the employee to do. The metric captures some dimensions of the desired behavior but misses others; it captures observable activity but not the underlying judgment, effort, or care that the activity is meant to reflect. The employee, responding rationally to the incentives the metric creates, optimizes against the metric — including in dimensions the firm did not intend and that may damage the firm's interests. The literature on performance measurement is, in important respects, the analytical study of this distortion.

This chapter develops the framework. The framework is, in its formal structure, a continuation of the moral hazard analysis of Chapter 4 — the agent has private information about her actions, the principal observes only a noisy signal of those actions, and the principal designs a contract that conditions on the available signals. The added complication is that the agent's actions are *multidimensional*: the agent allocates effort across multiple tasks, only some of which the principal can directly contract on. The classic statement is @holmstrommilgrom1991, "Multitask Principal-Agent Analyses," who showed that strong incentives on the measurable tasks predictably crowd out effort on the unmeasurable ones. The Wells Fargo case is that result in its most spectacular form.

The chapter introduces the standard internal performance metrics that firms use — return on investment (ROI), residual income (RI), economic value added (EVA), and the balanced scorecard — and develops each as a particular response to the multitasking problem. None of these metrics is a clean solution. Each has characteristic distortions of its own. The framework gives us a tool for predicting which distortions a given metric will produce, and the chapter's discussion questions and problems ask the student to apply the tool to specific design problems.

## The multitasking framework

Consider an extension of the LEN model from Chapter 4 in which the agent allocates effort across multiple tasks. Let the agent's effort vector be $\mathbf{e} = (e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_n)$, with each component $e_i$ representing effort on task $i$. The cost of effort is $C(\mathbf{e})$, an increasing convex function with substitution: time spent on task 1 is time not spent on tasks 2 through $n$. The firm's true preferences are a weighted combination of the tasks, with weights $b_1, \ldots, b_n$ that reflect what the firm actually values.

The contracting problem arises because the principal cannot directly observe $\mathbf{e}$. The principal observes only signals of the form $\mathbf{y}_i = e_i + \varepsilon_i$ for some subset of the tasks $i \in S$, where $S$ is the set of *contractible* tasks. For tasks $i \notin S$, the principal cannot construct a contract that directly rewards effort — she may know the agent is doing the work in some general sense, but no measurable signal can be tied to compensation.

The principal designs a linear contract on the observable signals, $w(\mathbf{y}) = a + \sum_{i \in S} \alpha_i y_i$. The agent then allocates effort to maximize her own certainty-equivalent payoff. Holmstrom and Milgrom show that the agent responds in a specific way: she supplies more effort to tasks with high $\alpha_i$ and less effort to the lightly-weighted tasks — including, critically, the tasks not contracted on at all because they are not observable.

The central result is that the optimal contract weights $\alpha_i$ are *not* simply the firm's underlying preferences $b_i$. The optimal weights account for the substitution patterns the agent will exhibit across tasks: if a high $\alpha_i$ on task 1 will cause the agent to underprovide effort on tasks 2 through $n$, the optimal contract may set $\alpha_1$ below $b_1$ to limit the distortion. In the extreme case where the unobservable tasks are sufficiently important, the optimal contract may set $\alpha_i = 0$ on the observable tasks — a flat wage — because incentivizing the observable tasks would produce a worse distortion in the unobservable tasks than the gain from incentivizing the observable tasks.

::: {.callout-note}
## The central insight

The Holmstrom-Milgrom result is often stated more simply: **incentivizing what you can measure crowds out what you cannot measure.** The Wells Fargo case is the canonical illustration. The bank could measure cross-sell counts cheaply and accurately; it could not, at low cost, measure customer-service quality, account integrity, or the absence of fraud. Incentivizing the measurable dimension produced a substitution away from the unmeasurable dimensions, with consequences that ultimately swamped the gains from the measured behavior.
:::

The multitasking framework has implications well beyond the Wells Fargo case. It predicts that any performance-measurement system using a narrow set of metrics will produce predictable distortions in the dimensions the metrics do not capture. (i) A teacher whose pay depends on test scores may neglect dimensions of student development the tests do not measure. (ii) An emergency-room physician evaluated on average length-of-stay may discharge patients prematurely. (iii) A salesperson whose commission is tied to closed deals may neglect customer relationships, follow-through, and account quality. The pattern is consistent across organizational settings, and the framework explains why. We will return to the empirical evidence in Section 12.7.

## Return on investment

The most widely-used internal performance metric in U.S. firms is *return on investment* (ROI) — operating profit divided by the assets deployed in the unit being measured:

$$
\text{ROI} = \frac{\text{Operating profit}}{\text{Average operating assets}}.
$$

The metric is intuitive: it reports the profit the unit produces per dollar of assets it controls. It is also comparable across units of different sizes, since the denominator scales with the unit's asset base. A division earning $50 million on $250 million of assets has an ROI of 20 percent; a division earning $200 million on $2 billion of assets has an ROI of 10 percent. ROI is widely used at investment-center boundaries (i.e., where the manager controls capital deployment) and in cross-divisional comparisons within multidivisional firms.

The ROI metric has, however, a well-known distortion. A manager whose performance is evaluated on ROI has an incentive to *underinvest* — specifically, to refuse projects whose ROI is below the unit's current ROI but above the firm's cost of capital. The mechanism is straightforward. A division currently earning 20 percent ROI is presented with a new investment opportunity that would earn 15 percent. The 15 percent return exceeds the firm's cost of capital (assume, say, 10 percent), so the project creates value for the firm. But accepting the project would *reduce* the division's average ROI from 20 percent toward 15 percent, which would harm the divisional manager's measured performance. The manager's rational response is to decline the project, even though the firm's interests favor accepting it.

The underinvestment distortion is the canonical problem of ROI as a performance metric. It is large enough in some settings that firms have moved to alternative metrics — particularly *residual income* and *economic value added* — which we develop next.

## Residual income, EVA, and a worked comparison

*Residual income* (RI) addresses the ROI underinvestment distortion by measuring the dollar amount of profit the unit produces in excess of a capital charge. The capital charge is the firm's required return times the assets deployed in the unit:

$$
\text{Residual income} = \text{Operating profit} - r \times \text{Average operating assets},
$$

where $r$ is the firm's required rate of return on the capital deployed.

Residual income has the property that any project whose return exceeds the cost of capital increases the unit's residual income. The 15 percent project from Section 12.3 would, under residual income, increase the unit's measured performance: the project earns more than the cost of capital, so it produces positive residual income. The underinvestment distortion is eliminated.

*Economic value added* (EVA), a trademark of the consulting firm Stern Stewart, is a refinement of residual income developed in the 1990s and adopted by a substantial number of large U.S. firms during that period. EVA differs from residual income in two specific respects. First, EVA adjusts the operating profit and asset base for items the firm believes are not informative about underlying performance — typically, (i) R&D is capitalized and amortized rather than expensed, (ii) operating leases are capitalized, (iii) goodwill amortization is added back, and (iv) deferred-tax adjustments are unwound. Second, EVA uses the firm's *weighted-average cost of capital* (WACC) — a blended return reflecting both equity and debt financing — rather than the cost of equity alone. The result is a metric that is, in principle, a closer approximation to the unit's economic value creation than residual income computed from unadjusted GAAP numbers.

### A worked comparison

Consider the Acme Imaging Division, an investment center within Acme Corp. The division's reported numbers for the most recent year are: operating profit of $40 million, average operating assets of $200 million, R&D expense of $15 million (treated by GAAP as a period cost), and an operating-lease commitment with a capitalized value of $30 million (off-balance-sheet under historical GAAP). Acme's cost of equity is 12 percent and its weighted-average cost of capital is 9 percent. The numbers are stylized but representative of a mid-sized industrial division.

**ROI.** The division's ROI is $40 / $200 = 20.0$ percent.

**Residual income.** Using the cost of equity as the capital charge, residual income equals $40 - 0.12 \times 200 = 40 - 24 = $16 million.

**EVA.** The EVA computation adjusts the operating profit and the asset base. To capitalize R&D, we add back the current-year R&D expense to operating profit and reduce it by the amortization of the capitalized R&D balance; assume the net effect on operating profit is $+$10 million (i.e., R&D capitalization produces a $10 million increase in adjusted operating profit). To capitalize the operating leases, we add the $30 million capitalized lease value to the asset base. The adjusted operating profit is $40 + 10 = $50 million; the adjusted asset base is $200 + 15 + 30 = $245 million, where the $15 million reflects the net capitalized R&D asset (current-year expense capitalized, less the prior amortization). EVA equals $50 - 0.09 \times 245 = 50 - 22.05 = $27.95 million.

The three metrics paint very different pictures of the same division. ROI reports a clean 20 percent return. Residual income reports $16 million of value creation above the equity-financing benchmark. EVA reports $27.95 million of value creation above the WACC benchmark, after correcting for the GAAP treatments Stern Stewart considers misleading. The metric a firm chooses is, in important respects, a choice about which set of distortions the firm is willing to tolerate.

Now consider the same division evaluating a new investment opportunity: a $50 million capital expenditure that would produce $7 million of incremental operating profit, with no associated R&D or leasing complications. The project's standalone ROI is $7 / $50 = 14.0$ percent. The project's residual income contribution is $7 - 0.12 \times 50 = 7 - 6 = +$1 million; the project's EVA contribution is $7 - 0.09 \times 50 = 7 - 4.5 = +$2.5 million.

A manager evaluated on ROI rejects the project: the project's 14 percent ROI is below the division's existing 20 percent ROI, and accepting the project would dilute the divisional average. A manager evaluated on residual income accepts the project: the $+$1 million RI contribution is positive. A manager evaluated on EVA accepts the project a fortiori: the $+$2.5 million EVA contribution is positive and larger than the RI contribution because the capital charge is lower under WACC than under the cost of equity.

The example makes concrete the broad claim of Sections 12.3 and 12.4: ROI produces a predictable underinvestment distortion that the dollar-based metrics correct, and the choice between residual income and EVA depends on how much of the GAAP-driven measurement noise the firm believes Stern Stewart-style adjustments actually correct.

::: {.callout-warning}
## Common pitfall

Residual income and EVA are sometimes presented as superior to ROI on the grounds that they "are correct" while ROI "is incorrect." The framing is too strong. Residual income and EVA correct the *underinvestment distortion* of ROI, but they introduce other distortions of their own. (i) A manager evaluated on residual income may overstate the unit's contribution to firm value by neglecting cross-divisional spillovers. (ii) A manager may resist asset sales that would reduce the capital charge even when the asset is genuinely impaired. (iii) The dollar-based metrics make small divisions look small in absolute terms, which can complicate compensation comparisons across units of different sizes. Each metric is the result of design choices, and each metric produces characteristic distortions. The right comparison is among the distortions, not between "right" and "wrong" metrics.
:::

## The balanced scorecard

The balanced scorecard, developed by @kaplannorton1992, is a non-quantitative response to the multitasking problem. Rather than pick a single metric and live with its distortions, the firm uses a *portfolio* of performance metrics covering multiple dimensions of the unit's activity. The original Kaplan-Norton framework identifies four perspectives: (i) *financial* (the traditional accounting metrics — ROI, residual income, EVA, growth in operating profit); (ii) *customer* (customer satisfaction, market share, retention, net promoter score); (iii) *internal business processes* (cycle times, defect rates, time-to-market for new products); and (iv) *learning and growth* (employee skill development, training hours, organizational capacity, employee retention).

The balanced-scorecard approach has been widely adopted in U.S. firms over the past three decades. The motivation, in the framework of Section 12.2, is that a portfolio of metrics covers more of the dimensions of the agent's effort than any single metric could; the multitasking distortions of any single metric are partially mitigated by the inclusion of other metrics capturing the dimensions the first metric missed. If a sales manager is evaluated on revenue alone, she has an incentive to maximize revenue at the expense of customer satisfaction; if she is evaluated on revenue and on a customer-satisfaction index, the substitution toward revenue-only behavior is dampened.

The balanced-scorecard approach has limitations of its own. (i) Adding metrics is easy; weighting them appropriately is hard. The literature has documented that, in practice, firms that adopt balanced-scorecard frameworks often revert to a small number of dominant metrics — typically the financial ones — when compensation decisions are made. The remaining metrics influence compensation only at the margin, if at all. (ii) Non-financial metrics are often constructed inside the firm, which means the manager has greater influence over the measurement process than for audited financial numbers. A customer-satisfaction score built from a survey the manager designs is not the same kind of signal as a residual-income number built from audited financial statements. (iii) Multiple metrics also create multiple gaming opportunities: a manager evaluated on five dimensions may game all five, more cheaply per dimension than she could game a single dimension that received heavier scrutiny.

@ittnerlarcker2003 reviewed the empirical evidence and concluded that the implementation of balanced-scorecard systems is harder than the conceptual framework suggests, and that many adopting firms do not realize the full benefits the framework predicts. Their finding is consistent with the broader pattern in this chapter: the balanced scorecard is a partial response to the multitasking problem, not a clean solution. The trade-off between simplicity and completeness in performance measurement does not have an obvious resolution; the right balance varies by firm, by industry, and by the specific roles being measured.

## Variance analysis

The chapter has so far focused on metrics that compare actual performance to a benchmark — the cost of capital, the firm's overall preferences, a portfolio of dimensions. A separate but related set of tools compares actual performance to a *budget* or *standard*, decomposing the difference into component pieces that identify where the deviation came from. This is the domain of *variance analysis*.

A *variance* is the difference between an actual value and a planned (i.e., budgeted or standard) value for a particular cost or revenue item. The standard decomposition produces three components. (i) A *price variance* captures the portion of the difference attributable to a difference between actual and standard prices. (ii) A *quantity variance* captures the portion attributable to a difference between actual and standard quantities consumed or produced. (iii) A *mix variance* captures the portion attributable to differences in the mix of products or inputs. The decomposition points the analyst toward the cause of the deviation: a cost overrun dominated by price variances suggests pursuing supplier renegotiations; one dominated by quantity variances suggests pursuing operational improvements; one dominated by mix variances suggests pursuing product-line review.

Variance analysis is a workhorse of internal management accounting. It is widely taught in introductory managerial accounting courses, and the mechanics are largely standardized across textbooks. We will not work through the formulas in detail here; the standard treatment is available in any intermediate textbook, and the problems for this chapter ask you to apply variance analysis to specific examples.

The framework of this chapter qualifies the value of variance analysis in a way the standard treatments often miss. A variance is a deviation from a benchmark, but the benchmark itself is a design choice. A standard cost set too tight produces unfavorable variances even when the firm is performing well; a standard cost set too loose produces favorable variances even when the firm is underperforming. The standard-setting process is itself subject to the multitasking distortions: a manager who negotiates her own standards has an incentive to negotiate loose ones, producing favorable variances at the cost of disguising underperformance. Variance analysis is most useful when the standards are credible — set by an outside party, indexed to external benchmarks, or otherwise made resistant to the manager's negotiating influence.

## Empirical evidence

The empirical literature on performance measurement is large; @ittnerlarcker2003 and the subsequent literature on management control systems provide useful entry points. Four findings are particularly relevant to the framework of this chapter.

First, the *consequences of multitasking distortions* are real and measurable in settings far beyond the Wells Fargo case. In health care, the introduction of publicly-reported surgical-mortality scorecards for cardiac surgeons in New York and Pennsylvania in the early 1990s was followed by a pronounced pattern of patient selection: surgeons whose mortality rates were being scored became more selective about which patients they would operate on, declining the sickest patients whose deaths would worsen the surgeon's reported numbers. The sickest patients were redirected to out-of-state hospitals or denied surgery altogether. The reported mortality rates improved, but at the cost of access for the patients most in need of care — a substitution toward the measurable dimension (reported mortality) and away from the unmeasurable dimension (treating the sickest patients). The pattern is consistent with the Holmstrom-Milgrom framework and has been documented across several empirical studies in the health-economics literature.

Second, the *school-accountability literature* documents a parallel pattern in education. The introduction of high-stakes standardized testing under No Child Left Behind (2001) and the state-level accountability systems that preceded it produced clear evidence of "teaching to the test" — substitution of instructional time toward tested subjects (reading and math) and away from untested subjects (science, history, art), substitution toward the specific question formats appearing on the tests, and, in extreme cases, outright cheating by teachers and administrators. The Chicago Public Schools test-tampering scandal of the early 2000s, documented at length in the economics literature, was the most striking case: statistical analysis of answer patterns revealed that a substantial fraction of teachers were systematically altering student answer sheets to inflate scores. The pattern of "what gets measured gets managed" — including its darker manifestations — is the multitasking framework in operation.

Third, *sales-quota gaming* is a recurring pattern in the corporate-incentive literature. The standard finding is that sales force compensation tied to quarterly quotas produces end-of-quarter pull-forwards (i.e., sales artificially advanced from the next quarter into the current quarter to clear the quota) and beginning-of-quarter pushbacks (i.e., sales artificially delayed when the current quarter is unsalvageable, to give the next quarter a head start). The pattern is observable in firm-level revenue time series and has been used in academic studies to identify the incidence of quota-based compensation in specific industries. The case study most often used in classroom settings is the Sears Auto Centers episode of 1992, in which mechanics evaluated on per-hour repair revenue were found to be recommending unnecessary repairs — substitution toward the measurable dimension (revenue per labor hour) and away from the unmeasurable dimension (only recommending repairs the customer actually needed).

Fourth, the *substitution from short-term to long-term metrics* in U.S. compensation contracts has been documented in recent decades. Many firms have moved from purely annual performance metrics toward mixes that include multi-year performance shares, relative-performance evaluation, and clawback provisions tied to long-term outcomes. The substitution is consistent with the prediction that the multitasking distortions of short-term metrics can be partially mitigated by introducing longer-horizon evaluations, though the empirical evidence on whether the change has actually reduced gaming is mixed.

The four findings together support the broad claim of this chapter: performance metrics produce real behavioral responses, and the responses include both the desired changes (more effort on the measured dimension) and the predictable distortions (less effort on the unmeasured dimensions). The framework of Section 12.2 is, by the standards of empirical economics, an unusually well-supported piece of theory.

::: {.callout-note}
## A case worth knowing: GE under Welch

General Electric under Jack Welch (CEO from 1981 to 2001) is the most-studied corporate case of single-metric-driven management. Welch ran GE on a small set of dominant financial metrics — operating margin, return on equity, and "Six Sigma" defect rates — and tied substantial divisional compensation to those metrics. The strategy produced two decades of extraordinary reported performance and made Welch one of the most-admired executives of his generation. It also produced, in retrospect, a set of distortions consistent with the multitasking framework. GE Capital grew into a financial-services giant in part because reported earnings from financial-services operations could be smoothed across periods in ways that operating-business earnings could not — the unit became, in important respects, an instrument for managing the firm's reported metrics. Several of GE's industrial businesses underinvested in research and capital expenditure during the Welch era, producing strong short-term margins at the cost of competitive position in the 2000s and 2010s. The post-Welch decline of GE is, in the framework of this chapter, exactly the pattern the multitasking theory predicts: extraordinary measured performance during the incentive regime, followed by the slow revelation of the dimensions that were not being measured.
:::

## Key takeaways

- **Every performance metric is an imperfect signal.** A metric captures some dimensions of desired behavior and misses others; the missed dimensions are where the distortion lives.
- **The multitasking result is the deepest theorem in incentive theory.** When effort is multidimensional and only some tasks are measurable, strong incentives on the measurable tasks predictably crowd out effort on the unmeasurable ones [@holmstrommilgrom1991].
- **ROI produces underinvestment.** A manager evaluated on ROI rejects positive-NPV projects that would dilute the divisional ROI average, even when those projects create value for the firm.
- **Residual income and EVA correct underinvestment but introduce their own distortions.** The choice among ROI, RI, and EVA is a choice among distortion profiles, not a choice between right and wrong metrics.
- **The balanced scorecard is a partial response, not a solution.** Adding non-financial metrics dampens the multitasking distortion of any single metric but adds weighting and gaming problems of its own [@kaplannorton1992; @ittnerlarcker2003].
- **What gets measured gets managed — for good and for ill.** Hospital scorecards, school accountability, and sales-quota gaming all show the same pattern: measurable dimensions improve, unmeasurable dimensions deteriorate, and the net welfare effect depends on the relative magnitudes.
:::

The memo should (i) identify three dimensions of effort the role requires, (ii) describe the metrics currently in use and the distortions they produce, and (iii) propose a revised metric portfolio. Adopt a measured, third-person professional tone throughout — the memo is a deliverable, not an internal monologue.

## Lab

The in-class activity for the second meeting of this week is a **performance-evaluation simulation** building on the existing `performance evaluation 2.xlsx` materials. You will be assigned a role as either a divisional manager or a corporate evaluator and will play out a sequence of compensation-design and effort-allocation decisions. The simulation makes the multitasking framework concrete by showing, in real time, how a particular performance metric induces predictable distortions in the manager's behavior.

There is no out-of-class lab for this week. The Lab 6 group empirical project is in active development; preliminary findings are due in Week 14.

## Further reading

The four readings below are the assigned readings for Week 12. You should read all of them.

- @holmstrommilgrom1991, "Multitask Principal-Agent Analyses." The foundational paper on the multitasking problem. Read the introduction and the simplest version of the model carefully; the full paper is technical and may be skimmed past the central result.
- @ittnerlarcker2003, "Coming Up Short on Nonfinancial Performance Measurement." A short and accessible review of the empirical evidence on balanced-scorecard adoption.
- A short selection from @demski2008 on responsibility accounting (chapter assignment to be specified on Canvas).
- *Optional, for those who want more:* @kaplannorton1992, the original balanced-scorecard paper, together with the subsequent literature on its empirical performance.

## Where Arc III goes from here

Chapter 12 has examined the design of performance measures for ongoing operations. Chapter 13 turns to the related but distinct problem of capital allocation: how the firm decides which long-term investments to make under conditions of asymmetric information about project quality. The framework of Chapters 4 and 12 returns explicitly, with the additional refinement that the agent's private information is now about *which projects to undertake* rather than about *how much effort to supply.*
