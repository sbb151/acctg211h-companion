8  Long-Term Assets and Measurement Choice

8.1 Opening question

At the close of trading on October 28, 2024, Apple Inc. had a market capitalization of approximately $3.4 trillion. The book value of Apple’s common equity, reported in its most recent quarterly filing, was approximately $66 billion. The gap is large enough that the market capitalized Apple at slightly more than fifty times the carrying amount of its equity, and the gap is concentrated almost entirely in items that do not appear on Apple’s balance sheet. The Apple brand is not on the balance sheet. The accumulated knowledge of Apple’s engineering and design teams is not on the balance sheet. The relationships Apple has built over decades with its suppliers, its customers, and its developer ecosystem are not on the balance sheet. None of these items qualifies for asset recognition under U.S. generally accepted accounting principles (i.e., GAAP), and yet on any reasonable accounting of where Apple’s economic value comes from, they are the bulk of the firm.

Apple is not unusual. Microsoft, Alphabet, NVIDIA, Meta, and Amazon all trade at market-to-book ratios substantially greater than one, and the bulk of each firm’s gap reflects items the U.S. accounting system does not recognize. The pattern extends past technology firms. Pharmaceutical firms, consumer-brand firms, and professional-services firms all exhibit market-to-book ratios that imply substantial unrecognized intangibles, and the aggregate market-to-book ratio of U.S. public companies has risen steadily over the past four decades (Lev and Gu 2016, who document that most of the rise is attributable to the rising importance of intangibles in modern corporate value).

The pattern raises a question that has occupied a substantial fraction of the empirical accounting literature. Should accounting recognize as assets the items that produce most of the modern firm’s value but that do not arise from arms-length transactions? If recognition is appropriate, how should those items be measured? If recognition is not appropriate, what is the cost of leaving them off the balance sheet? The question is a contemporary version of the trade-off between relevance and faithful representation that the FASB Conceptual Framework treats as the fundamental axis of measurement choice. Items like brand value, customer relationships, and accumulated research and development (i.e., R&D) spending are highly relevant to investors trying to value the firm. They are, however, difficult to measure faithfully: there is no arms-length transaction that establishes a verifiable price, and any internally-developed estimate sits at the manager’s discretion.

This chapter develops the framework for measuring long-term assets. It introduces the mechanics of property, plant, and equipment (i.e., PP&E) under U.S. GAAP — capitalization, depreciation, and impairment — and the corresponding mechanics for intangible assets, with particular attention to the asymmetric treatment of internally-developed and acquired intangibles. It introduces goodwill, the residual that arises in business combinations and that, since 2001, has been carried at cost subject only to impairment testing. It engages the long-running debate between Holthausen and Watts (2001), who argue that the value-relevance literature has limited use for standard setting, and Barth, Beaver, and Landsman (2001), who defend the value-relevance literature as one useful input among others. The chapter also points forward to WRDS Lab 3, in which students examine the cross-industry distribution of unrecognized intangibles in the spirit of Lev and Gu (2016).

8.2 The measurement debate: historical cost versus fair value

Before turning to mechanics, we set out the central measurement debate. U.S. GAAP measures most long-lived operating assets at historical cost less accumulated depreciation, with downward adjustments only when an impairment trigger is met. The alternative, in principle, is to remeasure these assets at fair value each period — to mark them up when the assets appreciate and down when they depreciate, with the change running through earnings or other comprehensive income. The choice between the two regimes is the most consequential measurement question in accounting for long-term assets, and the resolution embedded in current GAAP reflects a specific position in the relevance-versus-faithful-representation trade-off introduced in Chapter 5.

The case for fair value is the relevance case. A balance sheet that reports assets at their current fair value tells investors what those assets are worth today, which is the number an investor needs to value the firm. The historical cost of a Manhattan office building purchased in 1972 has essentially no relationship to the building’s economic value in 2026, and a balance sheet that carries the building at its 1972 cost provides little decision-useful information about the firm’s current position. The relevance argument is the same argument that animates the Lev and Gu (2016) critique of intangibles accounting and that informs the fair-value movement more broadly across financial reporting.

The case for historical cost is the faithful-representation case, sharpened by two related considerations: verifiability and stewardship. Verifiability, in the FASB’s qualitative-characteristics framework, refers to the capacity of independent observers to reach agreement about a measurement. Historical cost is verifiable because it is the outcome of an arms-length transaction with a counterparty: the price was negotiated, the cash changed hands, and the documentation persists. Fair value, by contrast, requires the firm to estimate the asset’s current value by reference to a market that may not exist (most PP&E is firm-specific and rarely traded) or by reference to discounted-cash-flow projections that depend on the manager’s assumptions about future operating performance. The verifiability of fair-value estimates for non-financial assets is, in most cases, considerably lower than the verifiability of historical cost.

Stewardship — the use of accounting numbers to evaluate management’s deployment of the resources entrusted to it — sharpens the case further. If management is evaluated against the historical cost of the assets it controls, the resulting performance measure is anchored to the investment decision that management actually made. If management is evaluated against fair value, the performance measure incorporates market-wide changes (e.g., real-estate price movements, interest-rate changes) that are not under management’s control. The Watts–Zimmerman positive-accounting framework of Chapter 5 predicts that contracting parties will demand verifiable, hard-to-manipulate measures for stewardship purposes, and historical cost satisfies this demand in a way that fair value does not. The conservatism arguments developed by Watts (2003), who provides four economic explanations for the persistence of conservatism in accounting (i.e., contracting, litigation, regulation, and taxation), apply directly: a measurement regime that admits unverifiable upward revisions creates an opportunity for opportunism that contracting parties may rationally choose to foreclose.

U.S. GAAP, on balance, weights faithful representation more heavily for operating assets, and the institutional choice that follows is historical cost with an asymmetric impairment regime. Downward adjustments are permitted (indeed required) when impairment indicators are met; upward adjustments back to fair value are not permitted, even when the underlying asset has appreciated. The asymmetry is conservatism in operation, and the empirical literature has documented that the asymmetry is a persistent and economically meaningful feature of U.S. accounting (Watts 2003, who treats the asymmetric verifiability standard as a central explanatory mechanism). International Financial Reporting Standards (i.e., IFRS) permit upward revaluation of PP&E in some circumstances under IAS 16, but firms reporting under IFRS rarely elect the revaluation option, suggesting that the demand for verifiable, conservative measurement is robust across reporting regimes.

Why this matters

The historical-cost choice is not a relic. It is an active institutional commitment to verifiability and stewardship, accepted in spite of the relevance cost. A reader who finds GAAP balance sheets “outdated” because they fail to reflect current asset values is correct on the relevance dimension but is missing the contracting and stewardship rationale on the faithful-representation dimension. The trade-off is genuine.

8.3 Property, plant, and equipment

We now turn to the mechanics, beginning with PP&E. Tangible long-lived assets — buildings, machinery, vehicles, equipment, leasehold improvements — are recorded at acquisition cost and depreciated over their useful lives, with the rules codified at ASC 360.

The acquisition cost includes all expenditures necessary to bring the asset to its intended use: (i) the purchase price; (ii) freight and installation costs; (iii) sales taxes; and (iv) for self-constructed assets, materials, labor, and capitalized interest associated with construction. Costs incurred after the asset is placed in service are typically expensed unless they extend the asset’s useful life or substantially increase its productive capacity, in which case they are capitalized as additions to the asset’s cost basis.

Once the asset is in service, it is depreciated. Depreciation is the systematic allocation of the asset’s cost (less an estimated salvage value) over its useful life. The depreciable base is cost less salvage value; the allocation method is the firm’s choice, subject to consistency and disclosure requirements. Three methods are dominant in practice.

Straight-line depreciation allocates the depreciable base evenly across the useful life. The annual expense is constant. The method is simple, comparable across firms, and produces a flat earnings effect.

Double-declining-balance depreciation (i.e., DDB) — and other accelerated methods — allocates more of the cost to the early years of the asset’s life and less to the later years. The method recognizes that many assets generate more service in early years (when they are new) than in later years (when they are partially depleted), and it produces lower book value and lower reported earnings in the early years relative to straight-line, with the difference reversing over the asset’s life.

Units-of-production depreciation (i.e., UoP) allocates the depreciable base in proportion to the asset’s actual output during the period. The method aligns expense with use but is rarely employed in practice because it requires output measurement and produces a more variable expense pattern than the alternatives.

The choice among methods has both real and informational consequences. The Watts–Zimmerman framework of Chapter 5 predicts that managers will tend to select methods consistent with their incentives — straight-line where higher early earnings are valuable for compensation or covenant purposes, accelerated where lower early earnings are valuable for tax or political-cost purposes. The empirical literature has documented patterns of method choice consistent with this prediction.

8.3.1 A worked depreciation example

Consider a $100{,}000 asset with a ten-year useful life and a $10{,}000 estimated salvage value. The depreciable base is $90{,}000.

Straight-line. The annual depreciation expense is \[ \text{Annual depreciation} = \frac{\$100{,}000 - \$10{,}000}{10} = \$9{,}000. \] The expense is $9{,}000 in each of the ten years. Accumulated depreciation grows by $9{,}000 per year, reaching $90{,}000 at the end of year ten. The asset is then carried at $10{,}000 (its salvage value) and is depreciated no further.

Double-declining-balance. The DDB rate is twice the straight-line rate: \(2 \times (1/10) = 20\%\). The rate is applied each year to the carrying amount (cost less accumulated depreciation), not to the depreciable base. Depreciation continues until the carrying amount equals salvage value, at which point it stops.

Units-of-production. Suppose the asset is expected to produce 1{,}000{,}000 units over its life, and actual output in the first three years is 150{,}000, 130{,}000, and 110{,}000 units, respectively. The per-unit depreciation rate is \[ \text{Rate} = \frac{\$100{,}000 - \$10{,}000}{1{,}000{,}000 \text{ units}} = \$0.09 \text{ per unit}. \] Year-one depreciation is $13{,}500 (i.e., \(150{,}000 \times \$0.09\)); year-two is $11{,}700; year-three is $9{,}900. The pattern tracks production and is not pre-specified.

The three methods produce identical lifetime depreciation (the depreciable base of $90{,}000) but very different annual patterns. Under straight-line, expense is constant and earnings are higher in early years (and lower in late years) than under DDB. Under DDB, expense is front-loaded and earnings are lower in early years (and higher in late years) than under straight-line. The choice affects the level of reported earnings, the carrying amount on the balance sheet at any intermediate date, and the timing — but not the total — of the expense recognized over the asset’s life. The cumulative cash flows are identical across methods, suggesting that, in efficient markets, the choice should not affect firm value. The empirical evidence is consistent with this prediction for most depreciation-method differences, although tax-method choices (which do produce real cash differences) are more value-relevant.

Common pitfall

Depreciation is sometimes confused with cash outflows. The cash outflow associated with PP&E occurs at acquisition (and at any subsequent capitalized expenditure); the depreciation expense recognized over the asset’s life is non-cash. The reconciliation in the indirect-method cash flow statement (Section 7.6) makes this explicit by adding depreciation back to net income. A firm whose earnings are depressed by depreciation charges may have substantial operating cash flow even when reported earnings are low.

8.4 Impairment of long-lived tangible assets

The depreciation schedule established at acquisition implicitly assumes that the asset’s pattern of economic benefits will follow the predetermined allocation. The world frequently does not cooperate. A factory expected to produce widgets for fifteen years may become obsolete after eight when the relevant technology shifts. A retail location expected to be productive may become unproductive when the surrounding neighborhood changes. A piece of equipment specific to a particular product line may lose its value if the product is discontinued. In these cases, the carrying amount on the balance sheet — historical cost less accumulated depreciation — overstates the asset’s economic value.

The institutional response is the impairment test. Under ASC 360, a long-lived tangible asset is tested for impairment whenever events or circumstances indicate that the asset’s carrying amount may not be recoverable. Triggering events include (i) a significant decrease in the asset’s market price, (ii) a significant adverse change in the way the asset is used, (iii) a significant adverse change in legal factors or business climate, (iv) an accumulation of costs significantly in excess of the amount originally expected, (v) operating or cash-flow losses associated with the asset, and (vi) a more-likely-than-not expectation that the asset will be sold or disposed of before the end of its previously estimated useful life.

The test has two steps. Step one is the recoverability test: the firm compares the asset’s carrying amount to the undiscounted cash flows expected to be derived from the asset over its remaining life. If the undiscounted cash flows exceed the carrying amount, no impairment is recognized. If the undiscounted cash flows fall short of the carrying amount, the firm proceeds to step two. Step two is the fair-value writedown: the firm computes the asset’s fair value (typically the present value of expected cash flows or, where available, an observable market price) and writes the asset down to fair value. The writedown is recognized as a loss on the income statement, and the post-impairment carrying amount becomes the new cost basis for subsequent depreciation.

The two-step structure is conservative in a specific sense. The test triggers impairment only when undiscounted cash flows fall below carrying amount, which is a high bar: an asset whose discounted cash flows are below carrying amount but whose undiscounted cash flows are above carrying amount escapes impairment under step one. The asymmetry reflects the FASB’s reluctance to permit upward revaluations and is consistent with the asymmetric verifiability standard of Watts (2003), who argues that contracting parties demand higher verification for gains than for losses precisely because gain-inflation is the opportunism most costly to creditors and other stewards.

A canonical example is Boeing’s PP&E impairment associated with the 737 MAX. Following the global grounding of the 737 MAX in 2019 and the subsequent reduction in expected production rates, Boeing recognized substantial charges related to the asset base supporting 737 MAX production — including inventory writedowns, accelerated depreciation on tooling, and impairment charges on related fixed assets. The Boeing case illustrates the standard impairment trigger pattern: an external event (the grounding) materially changed the expected cash flows from a defined asset group, the recoverability test was failed, and the resulting fair-value writedown ran through earnings in the period of recognition. The size of the charges, and their concentration in a small number of reporting periods, also illustrates the lumpy character of impairment under U.S. GAAP relative to a continuous fair-value remeasurement regime.

The Watts–Zimmerman framework predicts that the timing of impairment recognition is itself a managerial choice. A manager reluctant to take a loss may delay impairment as long as the undiscounted-cash-flow test can be argued to not yet be triggered. A manager seeking to clean the slate (the “big bath” pattern from Chapter 4) may accelerate impairment in a period in which other negative news is being absorbed. The empirical literature has documented both behaviors, and the timing of impairment is a contested area of accounting discretion to which we return in Section 8.7.

8.5 Intangible assets

The U.S. GAAP treatment of intangible assets is, on first encounter, surprising. The central asymmetry is the distinction between acquired and internally developed intangibles.

Intangibles that are acquired in arms-length transactions — through purchase, license, or business combination — are recognized as assets at acquisition cost (or, in the case of business combinations, at fair value at the acquisition date). Acquired intangibles include patents, copyrights, trademarks, customer lists, and licensing rights. Once recognized, acquired intangibles are treated in one of two ways depending on their useful life. Finite-lived intangibles (e.g., a ten-year patent or a five-year customer contract) are amortized over their useful lives on a straight-line basis and tested for impairment when triggering events arise. Indefinite-lived intangibles (e.g., a perpetual trademark or a broadcast license with no expiration) are not amortized under ASC 350; they are instead tested for impairment annually, with the test performed at the level of the reporting unit or asset group.

Intangibles that are internally developed — through R&D spending, marketing expenditures, training programs, or the accumulation of human capital — are, in most cases, expensed as incurred. Research and development costs are expensed under ASC 730 with narrow exceptions. The principal exception is internally-developed software: costs incurred after technological feasibility is established and before general release may be capitalized under ASC 985-20 (for software sold to third parties) or ASC 350-40 (for internal-use software). Marketing expenditures, training costs, and most product-development costs outside the software exception are expensed in the period incurred, regardless of the magnitude or expected duration of their economic benefits.

The asymmetry produces measurement gaps that are large in modern intangibles-intensive industries. Apple’s roughly $30 billion in annual R&D, Alphabet’s roughly $45 billion, and Microsoft’s roughly $30 billion are recorded as period expenses, even though substantial portions of these expenditures presumably create assets with multi-year economic lives. A firm that acquires a competitor with valuable patents recognizes the patents on its balance sheet; a firm that produces equally valuable patents through its own R&D spending does not. Two firms with identical underlying economic activity can therefore have very different balance sheets depending on the legal form through which their intangibles were obtained.

The asymmetry is not arbitrary. The verifiability concern is the same one that motivates historical cost for PP&E: an arms-length transaction establishes a verifiable price; an internally-developed asset requires the firm to estimate the asset’s value by reference to expected future cash flows. The FASB’s reluctance to permit capitalization of internally-developed intangibles reflects, in part, the construct-validity worry that any internally-developed estimate sits at the manager’s discretion. The conservatism principle, in the Watts (2003) formulation, tilts the balance toward expense recognition for internally-developed intangibles even at the cost of producing balance sheets that systematically understate the firm’s economic asset base.

IFRS departs from U.S. GAAP in one notable respect. Under IAS 38, development costs (i.e., expenditures incurred after the project has reached a defined feasibility threshold) are required to be capitalized when six specific conditions are met, while research costs are expensed. The IFRS distinction is operationally narrow — most R&D spending across the IFRS-reporting universe is still expensed because it does not meet the IAS 38 development criteria — but the conceptual posture is different from U.S. GAAP: IFRS permits, and indeed requires, capitalization in defined circumstances, while U.S. GAAP does not. The empirical literature has examined whether the IAS 38 distinction produces capitalized intangible balances that are economically meaningful, with mixed findings.

The cost of the asymmetric treatment, on the Lev and Gu (2016) view, is that financial statements have lost much of their usefulness for valuation purposes as the economy has shifted toward intangible-intensive production. A balance sheet that omits the assets that produce most of the firm’s economic value is an increasingly poor representation of the firm’s economic position. The Lev–Gu argument has not produced a change in U.S. GAAP, but it has been influential in the academic literature and has informed ongoing FASB deliberations on the treatment of intangibles.

Why this matters

The asymmetric treatment of intangibles is not a technicality. It is, on Lev–Gu’s evidence, the single largest source of measurement error in modern U.S. GAAP balance sheets. Empirical work in the value-relevance tradition has documented that book-value information has lost predictive power over time, while non-financial information about innovation activity, brand strength, and human capital has gained predictive power. The pattern is consistent with the claim that the current GAAP rules are systematically failing to capture what matters most for the modern firm.

8.6 Goodwill

Goodwill is a special category of intangible asset that arises only in business combinations. When one firm acquires another, U.S. GAAP requires the acquirer to allocate the purchase price across the identifiable assets and liabilities of the acquired firm at their fair values at the acquisition date. The residual — the excess of the purchase price over the fair value of identifiable net assets — is recorded as goodwill on the acquirer’s balance sheet.

Mechanically, the computation is as follows. Suppose firm A acquires firm B for $1{,}000 million. The fair values of B’s identifiable assets and liabilities at the acquisition date are: tangible assets $400 million, identifiable intangibles (patents, customer lists, trademarks) $300 million, and liabilities assumed $100 million. The fair value of identifiable net assets is $400 + 300 - 100 = \(600\) million. Goodwill recorded by A is the residual: \(1{,}000 - 600 = \$400\) million. The $400 million captures items that did not qualify for separate recognition — the assembled workforce, the going-concern value of the business, the synergies expected from combining the two firms, and any element of A’s overpayment for B. It is, in practice, a heterogeneous bundle of items whose only commonality is that they did not fit into any of the other categories of recognized assets.

Until 2001, goodwill was amortized over a period of up to forty years. Statement of Financial Accounting Standards No. 142 (i.e., SFAS 142, now codified at ASC 350), issued in 2001, eliminated goodwill amortization and replaced it with an annual impairment test. Under the impairment-only model, goodwill is carried on the balance sheet at its acquisition-date amount until and unless it is determined to be impaired, at which point it is written down with the loss recognized on the income statement. The current impairment test (post-2017 simplification under ASU 2017-04) compares the carrying amount of the reporting unit, including goodwill, to its fair value. If the carrying amount exceeds the fair value, an impairment loss is recognized for the difference, capped at the carrying amount of goodwill.

The 2001 change was controversial when it was made, and remains so. The proponents argued that arbitrary amortization bore no necessary relationship to the underlying economic erosion of acquired goodwill — a one-size-fits-all schedule that destroyed information rather than producing it. The critics argued that the impairment-only model created an opportunity for managers to delay loss recognition: a manager reluctant to admit that an acquisition had been overpriced could, under the impairment-only model, decline to declare the impairment and rely on the discretion embedded in the impairment test to support that determination.

The canonical example is the AOL–Time Warner merger. In 2001, AOL acquired Time Warner in a stock-for-stock transaction valued at approximately $165 billion, generating an enormous goodwill balance on the combined entity’s books. In 2002, the firm recognized a goodwill impairment charge of approximately $99 billion — at the time the largest accounting loss in U.S. corporate history — when the post-merger economic reality failed to match the acquisition-date expectations. The AOL–Time Warner case illustrates both the scale of the goodwill that can accumulate in large business combinations and the lumpy, after-the-fact character of impairment recognition under the post-2001 regime: the loss was real, the loss was large, and the loss was concentrated in a single reporting period after a multi-quarter delay during which the underlying economic deterioration was already visible to the capital markets.

The empirical literature has produced evidence on both sides of the 2001 change. Beatty and Weber (2006) document patterns in goodwill impairment timing consistent with managerial discretion, suggesting that impairments are clustered in periods in which other negative news is being absorbed and are systematically delayed in firms whose managers face contractual or career consequences from the loss recognition. Li and Sloan (2017), in a paper titled “Has Goodwill Accounting Gone Bad?,” argue that the impairment-only model has produced systematic overstatement of goodwill on U.S. balance sheets and that the cumulative magnitude of unrecognized goodwill impairments is substantial. The literature has not converged on a definitive verdict, but the broad pattern is that the 2001 change did, as the critics anticipated, give managers more discretion over the timing of loss recognition, and the use of that discretion has not always been benign.

A subtle implication

Goodwill is, in the framework of Chapter 5, a high-relevance, low-faithful-representation account. It is highly relevant because it captures a large fraction of the value of acquired businesses. It is low in faithful representation because the underlying components of goodwill — workforce value, synergies, going-concern value — are inherently difficult to verify. The post-2001 impairment-only model is a particular institutional choice in the trade-off, and the choice has both vindicators and critics.

8.7 The value-relevance debate and the empirical evidence on impairment

The 2001 special issue of the Journal of Accounting and Economics contained two papers that have framed the academic debate over the value relevance of accounting numbers. Holthausen and Watts (2001), in a paper titled “The Relevance of the Value-Relevance Literature for Financial Accounting Standard Setting,” argue that the value-relevance literature has limited usefulness for standard-setting decisions. They identify several reasons. First, value-relevance studies typically demonstrate associations between accounting numbers and prices but do not establish that the accounting numbers cause the price reactions; the associations could reflect common dependence on underlying economic events that the accounting numbers and prices both reflect. Second, value-relevance studies frequently use aggregated measures that conflate the relevance of the accounting standard with the relevance of the underlying economic events being reported, making it difficult to extract guidance for specific standard-setting choices. Third, value-relevance studies are typically conducted on samples that pre-date the standard-setting decision being evaluated, raising concerns about external validity. Holthausen and Watts argue, in light of these concerns, that the standard setter should rely on a broader and more conceptual analysis rather than on value-relevance evidence alone.

Barth, Beaver, and Landsman (2001), in a paper published in the same issue, defend the value-relevance literature. They argue that the literature does provide useful evidence for standard setters, particularly when the empirical design isolates the effects of specific accounting choices and when the inference is appropriately qualified. Value-relevance evidence is one input into the standard-setting decision, on their view, not the sole basis for it. The Barth–Beaver–Landsman position has been more influential within the empirical-accounting subfield, while the Holthausen–Watts position has been more influential among standard setters and in policy-oriented discussions. The two papers do not principally disagree about the empirical evidence; they disagree about the inferences that can be drawn from it and about the role of empirical evidence in the standard-setting process.

The impairment literature speaks directly to this debate. Empirical work has documented that the timing of impairment recognition is, on average, partially driven by the underlying economics of the asset (i.e., impairments cluster in periods of adverse industry shocks and operating losses, consistent with the standard’s stated purpose) but also partially driven by managerial discretion. Beatty and Weber (2006) examine goodwill impairment decisions immediately following SFAS 142 and report that firms with debt covenants tied to net worth, firms with earnings-based bonus plans, and firms whose CEOs face career consequences from loss recognition are systematically slower to impair, consistent with the prediction that contracting incentives delay loss recognition. Li and Sloan (2017) extend the analysis through more recent vintages and document that the cumulative undisclosed goodwill impairments under the post-2001 regime are economically meaningful at the aggregate level, suggesting that delayed recognition produces a stock of impaired goodwill that is not reflected on contemporaneous balance sheets.

The empirical pattern is consistent with both interpretations of the regime. The impairment standard does identify some impairments in a timely way (the standard works), and the impairment standard also leaves substantial room for managerial discretion that is, on average, used to delay loss recognition (the standard is incomplete). The evidence is suggestive but not definitive on the question of whether the post-2001 regime is superior to the pre-2001 amortization regime. The amortization model had its own problems — arbitrary allocations, no responsiveness to the underlying economics of individual goodwill balances — and the empirical record on a regime-versus-regime comparison is mixed. The literature has not produced a consensus recommendation for a return to amortization, but it has produced sustained scrutiny of the post-2001 model and continued attention to the topic in FASB deliberations.

Truck, net $41,000

Suppose, three years into the truck’s life, Acme replaces the truck’s transmission for $3{,}000. The expenditure extends the truck’s expected useful life by one year. Under U.S. GAAP, the expenditure is a capital improvement — it is added to the truck’s cost basis, and the depreciation schedule is revised prospectively to reflect the new useful life and the new depreciable base.

Suppose, alternatively, that in year four Acme determines that the truck has become obsolete due to a shift in the firm’s business and is no longer expected to generate cash flows sufficient to recover its remaining carrying amount. Acme would test the truck for impairment under ASC 360. Assume the carrying amount at the end of year four is $14{,}000 (cost of $50{,}000 less four years of straight-line depreciation at $9{,}000 per year). Suppose the truck’s remaining undiscounted cash flows are estimated at $11{,}000 and its fair value (the present value of expected future cash flows) is estimated at $8{,}000. The recoverability test fails because the undiscounted cash flows ($11{,}000) are below the carrying amount ($14{,}000). Step two writes the truck down to fair value, producing a $6{,}000 impairment loss ($14{,}000 less $8{,}000). The new carrying amount becomes the basis for subsequent depreciation over the truck’s remaining useful life.

The example is straightforward. It is included here because the underlying mechanics — capitalize at cost, depreciate over useful life, capitalize subsequent improvements, test for impairment when triggered — are the workhorse of long-term asset accounting under U.S. GAAP. Deeper applications (component depreciation under IFRS, complex leasing arrangements, asset retirement obligations) are available in any intermediate accounting textbook, and the SEC’s staff accounting bulletins are the most authoritative source for narrow interpretive questions.

8.9 Key takeaways

Key takeaways
  • Historical cost is a stewardship choice, not an oversight. U.S. GAAP carries most long-lived operating assets at historical cost (less accumulated depreciation) because verifiability and stewardship considerations outweigh the relevance gain from continuous fair-value remeasurement.
  • Depreciation allocates cost, not value. Straight-line, double-declining-balance, and units-of-production methods produce identical lifetime depreciation on the same depreciable base, but they differ in the timing of expense recognition and therefore in the year-to-year pattern of reported earnings and carrying amount.
  • Impairment is a one-way ratchet. Long-lived tangible assets are written down to fair value when undiscounted cash flows fall below carrying amount, but they are not written back up when the asset’s economics improve — an asymmetry consistent with the conservatism principle of Watts (2003).
  • Intangibles are treated asymmetrically. Acquired intangibles are recognized at cost or fair value and either amortized or tested for impairment annually under ASC 350; internally-developed intangibles are largely expensed as incurred under ASC 730, leaving a measurement gap that has grown with the intangibles-intensive economy.
  • Goodwill is the residual that captures what survives. Goodwill arises only in business combinations as the excess of purchase price over the fair value of identifiable net assets, is not amortized post-SFAS 142, and is tested for impairment annually — a regime that gives managers discretion that the empirical literature finds is sometimes used to delay loss recognition.
  • The empirical record on impairment is mixed. Impairments are sometimes timely and economically driven (e.g., Boeing’s 737 MAX charges); impairments are sometimes delayed in ways consistent with managerial incentives (e.g., the empirical patterns in Beatty and Weber (2006) and Li and Sloan (2017)).

# Long-Term Assets and Measurement Choice {#sec-lta}

## Opening question

At the close of trading on October 28, 2024, Apple Inc. had a market capitalization of approximately $3.4 trillion. The book value of Apple's common equity, reported in its most recent quarterly filing, was approximately $66 billion. The gap is large enough that the market capitalized Apple at slightly more than fifty times the carrying amount of its equity, and the gap is concentrated almost entirely in items that do not appear on Apple's balance sheet. The Apple brand is not on the balance sheet. The accumulated knowledge of Apple's engineering and design teams is not on the balance sheet. The relationships Apple has built over decades with its suppliers, its customers, and its developer ecosystem are not on the balance sheet. None of these items qualifies for asset recognition under U.S. generally accepted accounting principles (i.e., GAAP), and yet on any reasonable accounting of where Apple's economic value comes from, they are the bulk of the firm.

Apple is not unusual. Microsoft, Alphabet, NVIDIA, Meta, and Amazon all trade at market-to-book ratios substantially greater than one, and the bulk of each firm's gap reflects items the U.S. accounting system does not recognize. The pattern extends past technology firms. Pharmaceutical firms, consumer-brand firms, and professional-services firms all exhibit market-to-book ratios that imply substantial unrecognized intangibles, and the aggregate market-to-book ratio of U.S. public companies has risen steadily over the past four decades [@levgu2016, who document that most of the rise is attributable to the rising importance of intangibles in modern corporate value].

The pattern raises a question that has occupied a substantial fraction of the empirical accounting literature. Should accounting recognize as assets the items that produce most of the modern firm's value but that do not arise from arms-length transactions? If recognition is appropriate, how should those items be measured? If recognition is not appropriate, what is the cost of leaving them off the balance sheet? The question is a contemporary version of the trade-off between relevance and faithful representation that the FASB Conceptual Framework treats as the fundamental axis of measurement choice. Items like brand value, customer relationships, and accumulated research and development (i.e., R&D) spending are highly relevant to investors trying to value the firm. They are, however, difficult to measure faithfully: there is no arms-length transaction that establishes a verifiable price, and any internally-developed estimate sits at the manager's discretion.

This chapter develops the framework for measuring long-term assets. It introduces the mechanics of property, plant, and equipment (i.e., PP&E) under U.S. GAAP — capitalization, depreciation, and impairment — and the corresponding mechanics for intangible assets, with particular attention to the asymmetric treatment of internally-developed and acquired intangibles. It introduces goodwill, the residual that arises in business combinations and that, since 2001, has been carried at cost subject only to impairment testing. It engages the long-running debate between @holthausenwatts2001, who argue that the value-relevance literature has limited use for standard setting, and @barthbeaverlandsman2001, who defend the value-relevance literature as one useful input among others. The chapter also points forward to WRDS Lab 3, in which students examine the cross-industry distribution of unrecognized intangibles in the spirit of @levgu2016.

## The measurement debate: historical cost versus fair value

Before turning to mechanics, we set out the central measurement debate. U.S. GAAP measures most long-lived operating assets at historical cost less accumulated depreciation, with downward adjustments only when an impairment trigger is met. The alternative, in principle, is to remeasure these assets at fair value each period — to mark them up when the assets appreciate and down when they depreciate, with the change running through earnings or other comprehensive income. The choice between the two regimes is the most consequential measurement question in accounting for long-term assets, and the resolution embedded in current GAAP reflects a specific position in the relevance-versus-faithful-representation trade-off introduced in Chapter 5.

The case for fair value is the relevance case. A balance sheet that reports assets at their current fair value tells investors what those assets are worth today, which is the number an investor needs to value the firm. The historical cost of a Manhattan office building purchased in 1972 has essentially no relationship to the building's economic value in 2026, and a balance sheet that carries the building at its 1972 cost provides little decision-useful information about the firm's current position. The relevance argument is the same argument that animates the @levgu2016 critique of intangibles accounting and that informs the fair-value movement more broadly across financial reporting.

The case for historical cost is the faithful-representation case, sharpened by two related considerations: verifiability and stewardship. Verifiability, in the FASB's qualitative-characteristics framework, refers to the capacity of independent observers to reach agreement about a measurement. Historical cost is verifiable because it is the outcome of an arms-length transaction with a counterparty: the price was negotiated, the cash changed hands, and the documentation persists. Fair value, by contrast, requires the firm to estimate the asset's current value by reference to a market that may not exist (most PP&E is firm-specific and rarely traded) or by reference to discounted-cash-flow projections that depend on the manager's assumptions about future operating performance. The verifiability of fair-value estimates for non-financial assets is, in most cases, considerably lower than the verifiability of historical cost.

Stewardship — the use of accounting numbers to evaluate management's deployment of the resources entrusted to it — sharpens the case further. If management is evaluated against the historical cost of the assets it controls, the resulting performance measure is anchored to the investment decision that management actually made. If management is evaluated against fair value, the performance measure incorporates market-wide changes (e.g., real-estate price movements, interest-rate changes) that are not under management's control. The Watts–Zimmerman positive-accounting framework of Chapter 5 predicts that contracting parties will demand verifiable, hard-to-manipulate measures for stewardship purposes, and historical cost satisfies this demand in a way that fair value does not. The conservatism arguments developed by @watts2003a, who provides four economic explanations for the persistence of conservatism in accounting (i.e., contracting, litigation, regulation, and taxation), apply directly: a measurement regime that admits unverifiable upward revisions creates an opportunity for opportunism that contracting parties may rationally choose to foreclose.

U.S. GAAP, on balance, weights faithful representation more heavily for operating assets, and the institutional choice that follows is historical cost with an asymmetric impairment regime. Downward adjustments are permitted (indeed required) when impairment indicators are met; upward adjustments back to fair value are not permitted, even when the underlying asset has appreciated. The asymmetry is conservatism in operation, and the empirical literature has documented that the asymmetry is a persistent and economically meaningful feature of U.S. accounting [@watts2003a, who treats the asymmetric verifiability standard as a central explanatory mechanism]. International Financial Reporting Standards (i.e., IFRS) permit upward revaluation of PP&E in some circumstances under IAS 16, but firms reporting under IFRS rarely elect the revaluation option, suggesting that the demand for verifiable, conservative measurement is robust across reporting regimes.

::: {.callout-note}
## Why this matters

The historical-cost choice is not a relic. It is an active institutional commitment to verifiability and stewardship, accepted in spite of the relevance cost. A reader who finds GAAP balance sheets "outdated" because they fail to reflect current asset values is correct on the relevance dimension but is missing the contracting and stewardship rationale on the faithful-representation dimension. The trade-off is genuine.
:::

## Property, plant, and equipment

We now turn to the mechanics, beginning with PP&E. Tangible long-lived assets — buildings, machinery, vehicles, equipment, leasehold improvements — are recorded at acquisition cost and depreciated over their useful lives, with the rules codified at ASC 360.

The acquisition cost includes all expenditures necessary to bring the asset to its intended use: (i) the purchase price; (ii) freight and installation costs; (iii) sales taxes; and (iv) for self-constructed assets, materials, labor, and capitalized interest associated with construction. Costs incurred after the asset is placed in service are typically expensed unless they extend the asset's useful life or substantially increase its productive capacity, in which case they are capitalized as additions to the asset's cost basis.

Once the asset is in service, it is depreciated. *Depreciation* is the systematic allocation of the asset's cost (less an estimated salvage value) over its useful life. The depreciable base is cost less salvage value; the allocation method is the firm's choice, subject to consistency and disclosure requirements. Three methods are dominant in practice.

*Straight-line depreciation* allocates the depreciable base evenly across the useful life. The annual expense is constant. The method is simple, comparable across firms, and produces a flat earnings effect.

*Double-declining-balance depreciation* (i.e., DDB) — and other accelerated methods — allocates more of the cost to the early years of the asset's life and less to the later years. The method recognizes that many assets generate more service in early years (when they are new) than in later years (when they are partially depleted), and it produces lower book value and lower reported earnings in the early years relative to straight-line, with the difference reversing over the asset's life.

*Units-of-production depreciation* (i.e., UoP) allocates the depreciable base in proportion to the asset's actual output during the period. The method aligns expense with use but is rarely employed in practice because it requires output measurement and produces a more variable expense pattern than the alternatives.

The choice among methods has both real and informational consequences. The Watts–Zimmerman framework of Chapter 5 predicts that managers will tend to select methods consistent with their incentives — straight-line where higher early earnings are valuable for compensation or covenant purposes, accelerated where lower early earnings are valuable for tax or political-cost purposes. The empirical literature has documented patterns of method choice consistent with this prediction.

### 8.3.1 A worked depreciation example

Consider a $100{,}000 asset with a ten-year useful life and a $10{,}000 estimated salvage value. The depreciable base is $90{,}000.

*Straight-line.* The annual depreciation expense is
$$
\text{Annual depreciation} = \frac{\$100{,}000 - \$10{,}000}{10} = \$9{,}000.
$$
The expense is $9{,}000 in each of the ten years. Accumulated depreciation grows by $9{,}000 per year, reaching $90{,}000 at the end of year ten. The asset is then carried at $10{,}000 (its salvage value) and is depreciated no further.

*Double-declining-balance.* The DDB rate is twice the straight-line rate: $2 \times (1/10) = 20\%$. The rate is applied each year to the *carrying amount* (cost less accumulated depreciation), not to the depreciable base. Depreciation continues until the carrying amount equals salvage value, at which point it stops.

| Year | Beginning carrying amount | DDB expense (20%) | Ending carrying amount |
|------|---------------------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| 1    | 100,000                   | 20,000            | 80,000                 |
| 2    | 80,000                    | 16,000            | 64,000                 |
| 3    | 64,000                    | 12,800            | 51,200                 |
| 4    | 51,200                    | 10,240            | 40,960                 |
| 5    | 40,960                    | 8,192             | 32,768                 |
| 6    | 32,768                    | 6,554             | 26,215                 |
| 7    | 26,215                    | 5,243             | 20,972                 |
| 8    | 20,972                    | 4,194             | 16,777                 |
| 9    | 16,777                    | 3,355             | 13,422                 |
| 10   | 13,422                    | 3,422             | 10,000                 |

Note that the year-ten expense is forced to $3{,}422 (rather than $2{,}684, i.e., 20\% of the beginning carrying amount) so that the ending carrying amount equals the salvage value of $10{,}000. In practice, firms often switch from DDB to straight-line in the year in which the straight-line expense on the remaining depreciable base exceeds the DDB expense; the switch produces a slightly different schedule but the same ten-year-end carrying amount.

*Units-of-production.* Suppose the asset is expected to produce 1{,}000{,}000 units over its life, and actual output in the first three years is 150{,}000, 130{,}000, and 110{,}000 units, respectively. The per-unit depreciation rate is
$$
\text{Rate} = \frac{\$100{,}000 - \$10{,}000}{1{,}000{,}000 \text{ units}} = \$0.09 \text{ per unit}.
$$
Year-one depreciation is $13{,}500 (i.e., $150{,}000 \times \$0.09$); year-two is $11{,}700; year-three is $9{,}900. The pattern tracks production and is not pre-specified.

The three methods produce identical *lifetime* depreciation (the depreciable base of $90{,}000) but very different annual patterns. Under straight-line, expense is constant and earnings are higher in early years (and lower in late years) than under DDB. Under DDB, expense is front-loaded and earnings are lower in early years (and higher in late years) than under straight-line. The choice affects the level of reported earnings, the carrying amount on the balance sheet at any intermediate date, and the timing — but not the total — of the expense recognized over the asset's life. The cumulative cash flows are identical across methods, suggesting that, in efficient markets, the choice should not affect firm value. The empirical evidence is consistent with this prediction for most depreciation-method differences, although tax-method choices (which do produce real cash differences) are more value-relevant.

## Common pitfall

Depreciation is sometimes confused with cash outflows. The cash outflow associated with PP&E occurs at acquisition (and at any subsequent capitalized expenditure); the depreciation expense recognized over the asset's life is non-cash. The reconciliation in the indirect-method cash flow statement (Section 7.6) makes this explicit by adding depreciation back to net income. A firm whose earnings are depressed by depreciation charges may have substantial operating cash flow even when reported earnings are low.

## Impairment of long-lived tangible assets

The depreciation schedule established at acquisition implicitly assumes that the asset's pattern of economic benefits will follow the predetermined allocation. The world frequently does not cooperate. A factory expected to produce widgets for fifteen years may become obsolete after eight when the relevant technology shifts. A retail location expected to be productive may become unproductive when the surrounding neighborhood changes. A piece of equipment specific to a particular product line may lose its value if the product is discontinued. In these cases, the carrying amount on the balance sheet — historical cost less accumulated depreciation — overstates the asset's economic value.

The institutional response is the *impairment test*. Under ASC 360, a long-lived tangible asset is tested for impairment whenever events or circumstances indicate that the asset's carrying amount may not be recoverable. Triggering events include (i) a significant decrease in the asset's market price, (ii) a significant adverse change in the way the asset is used, (iii) a significant adverse change in legal factors or business climate, (iv) an accumulation of costs significantly in excess of the amount originally expected, (v) operating or cash-flow losses associated with the asset, and (vi) a more-likely-than-not expectation that the asset will be sold or disposed of before the end of its previously estimated useful life.

The test has two steps. *Step one is the recoverability test:* the firm compares the asset's carrying amount to the *undiscounted* cash flows expected to be derived from the asset over its remaining life. If the undiscounted cash flows exceed the carrying amount, no impairment is recognized. If the undiscounted cash flows fall short of the carrying amount, the firm proceeds to step two. *Step two is the fair-value writedown:* the firm computes the asset's fair value (typically the present value of expected cash flows or, where available, an observable market price) and writes the asset down to fair value. The writedown is recognized as a loss on the income statement, and the post-impairment carrying amount becomes the new cost basis for subsequent depreciation.

The two-step structure is conservative in a specific sense. The test triggers impairment only when undiscounted cash flows fall below carrying amount, which is a high bar: an asset whose discounted cash flows are below carrying amount but whose undiscounted cash flows are above carrying amount escapes impairment under step one. The asymmetry reflects the FASB's reluctance to permit upward revaluations and is consistent with the asymmetric verifiability standard of @watts2003a, who argues that contracting parties demand higher verification for gains than for losses precisely because gain-inflation is the opportunism most costly to creditors and other stewards.

A canonical example is Boeing's PP&E impairment associated with the 737 MAX. Following the global grounding of the 737 MAX in 2019 and the subsequent reduction in expected production rates, Boeing recognized substantial charges related to the asset base supporting 737 MAX production — including inventory writedowns, accelerated depreciation on tooling, and impairment charges on related fixed assets. The Boeing case illustrates the standard impairment trigger pattern: an external event (the grounding) materially changed the expected cash flows from a defined asset group, the recoverability test was failed, and the resulting fair-value writedown ran through earnings in the period of recognition. The size of the charges, and their concentration in a small number of reporting periods, also illustrates the lumpy character of impairment under U.S. GAAP relative to a continuous fair-value remeasurement regime.

The Watts–Zimmerman framework predicts that the timing of impairment recognition is itself a managerial choice. A manager reluctant to take a loss may delay impairment as long as the undiscounted-cash-flow test can be argued to not yet be triggered. A manager seeking to clean the slate (the "big bath" pattern from Chapter 4) may accelerate impairment in a period in which other negative news is being absorbed. The empirical literature has documented both behaviors, and the timing of impairment is a contested area of accounting discretion to which we return in Section 8.7.

## Intangible assets

The U.S. GAAP treatment of intangible assets is, on first encounter, surprising. The central asymmetry is the distinction between *acquired* and *internally developed* intangibles.

Intangibles that are acquired in arms-length transactions — through purchase, license, or business combination — are recognized as assets at acquisition cost (or, in the case of business combinations, at fair value at the acquisition date). Acquired intangibles include patents, copyrights, trademarks, customer lists, and licensing rights. Once recognized, acquired intangibles are treated in one of two ways depending on their useful life. *Finite-lived intangibles* (e.g., a ten-year patent or a five-year customer contract) are amortized over their useful lives on a straight-line basis and tested for impairment when triggering events arise. *Indefinite-lived intangibles* (e.g., a perpetual trademark or a broadcast license with no expiration) are *not amortized* under ASC 350; they are instead tested for impairment annually, with the test performed at the level of the reporting unit or asset group.

Intangibles that are *internally developed* — through R&D spending, marketing expenditures, training programs, or the accumulation of human capital — are, in most cases, expensed as incurred. Research and development costs are expensed under ASC 730 with narrow exceptions. The principal exception is internally-developed software: costs incurred after technological feasibility is established and before general release may be capitalized under ASC 985-20 (for software sold to third parties) or ASC 350-40 (for internal-use software). Marketing expenditures, training costs, and most product-development costs outside the software exception are expensed in the period incurred, regardless of the magnitude or expected duration of their economic benefits.

The asymmetry produces measurement gaps that are large in modern intangibles-intensive industries. Apple's roughly $30 billion in annual R&D, Alphabet's roughly $45 billion, and Microsoft's roughly $30 billion are recorded as period expenses, even though substantial portions of these expenditures presumably create assets with multi-year economic lives. A firm that acquires a competitor with valuable patents recognizes the patents on its balance sheet; a firm that produces equally valuable patents through its own R&D spending does not. Two firms with identical underlying economic activity can therefore have very different balance sheets depending on the legal form through which their intangibles were obtained.

The asymmetry is not arbitrary. The verifiability concern is the same one that motivates historical cost for PP&E: an arms-length transaction establishes a verifiable price; an internally-developed asset requires the firm to estimate the asset's value by reference to expected future cash flows. The FASB's reluctance to permit capitalization of internally-developed intangibles reflects, in part, the construct-validity worry that any internally-developed estimate sits at the manager's discretion. The conservatism principle, in the @watts2003a formulation, tilts the balance toward expense recognition for internally-developed intangibles even at the cost of producing balance sheets that systematically understate the firm's economic asset base.

IFRS departs from U.S. GAAP in one notable respect. Under IAS 38, *development* costs (i.e., expenditures incurred after the project has reached a defined feasibility threshold) are required to be capitalized when six specific conditions are met, while *research* costs are expensed. The IFRS distinction is operationally narrow — most R&D spending across the IFRS-reporting universe is still expensed because it does not meet the IAS 38 development criteria — but the conceptual posture is different from U.S. GAAP: IFRS permits, and indeed requires, capitalization in defined circumstances, while U.S. GAAP does not. The empirical literature has examined whether the IAS 38 distinction produces capitalized intangible balances that are economically meaningful, with mixed findings.

The cost of the asymmetric treatment, on the @levgu2016 view, is that financial statements have lost much of their usefulness for valuation purposes as the economy has shifted toward intangible-intensive production. A balance sheet that omits the assets that produce most of the firm's economic value is an increasingly poor representation of the firm's economic position. The Lev–Gu argument has not produced a change in U.S. GAAP, but it has been influential in the academic literature and has informed ongoing FASB deliberations on the treatment of intangibles.

::: {.callout-note}
## Why this matters

The asymmetric treatment of intangibles is not a technicality. It is, on Lev–Gu's evidence, the single largest source of measurement error in modern U.S. GAAP balance sheets. Empirical work in the value-relevance tradition has documented that book-value information has lost predictive power over time, while non-financial information about innovation activity, brand strength, and human capital has gained predictive power. The pattern is consistent with the claim that the current GAAP rules are systematically failing to capture what matters most for the modern firm.
:::

## Goodwill

Goodwill is a special category of intangible asset that arises only in business combinations. When one firm acquires another, U.S. GAAP requires the acquirer to allocate the purchase price across the identifiable assets and liabilities of the acquired firm at their fair values at the acquisition date. The residual — the excess of the purchase price over the fair value of identifiable net assets — is recorded as *goodwill* on the acquirer's balance sheet.

Mechanically, the computation is as follows. Suppose firm A acquires firm B for $1{,}000 million. The fair values of B's identifiable assets and liabilities at the acquisition date are: tangible assets $400 million, identifiable intangibles (patents, customer lists, trademarks) $300 million, and liabilities assumed $100 million. The fair value of identifiable net assets is $400 + 300 - 100 = $600$ million. Goodwill recorded by A is the residual: $1{,}000 - 600 = \$400$ million. The $400 million captures items that did not qualify for separate recognition — the assembled workforce, the going-concern value of the business, the synergies expected from combining the two firms, and any element of A's overpayment for B. It is, in practice, a heterogeneous bundle of items whose only commonality is that they did not fit into any of the other categories of recognized assets.

Until 2001, goodwill was amortized over a period of up to forty years. *Statement of Financial Accounting Standards No. 142* (i.e., SFAS 142, now codified at ASC 350), issued in 2001, eliminated goodwill amortization and replaced it with an annual impairment test. Under the impairment-only model, goodwill is carried on the balance sheet at its acquisition-date amount until and unless it is determined to be impaired, at which point it is written down with the loss recognized on the income statement. The current impairment test (post-2017 simplification under ASU 2017-04) compares the carrying amount of the reporting unit, including goodwill, to its fair value. If the carrying amount exceeds the fair value, an impairment loss is recognized for the difference, capped at the carrying amount of goodwill.

The 2001 change was controversial when it was made, and remains so. The proponents argued that arbitrary amortization bore no necessary relationship to the underlying economic erosion of acquired goodwill — a one-size-fits-all schedule that destroyed information rather than producing it. The critics argued that the impairment-only model created an opportunity for managers to delay loss recognition: a manager reluctant to admit that an acquisition had been overpriced could, under the impairment-only model, decline to declare the impairment and rely on the discretion embedded in the impairment test to support that determination.

The canonical example is the AOL–Time Warner merger. In 2001, AOL acquired Time Warner in a stock-for-stock transaction valued at approximately $165 billion, generating an enormous goodwill balance on the combined entity's books. In 2002, the firm recognized a goodwill impairment charge of approximately $99 billion — at the time the largest accounting loss in U.S. corporate history — when the post-merger economic reality failed to match the acquisition-date expectations. The AOL–Time Warner case illustrates both the scale of the goodwill that can accumulate in large business combinations and the lumpy, after-the-fact character of impairment recognition under the post-2001 regime: the loss was real, the loss was large, and the loss was concentrated in a single reporting period after a multi-quarter delay during which the underlying economic deterioration was already visible to the capital markets.

The empirical literature has produced evidence on both sides of the 2001 change. @beattyweber2006 document patterns in goodwill impairment timing consistent with managerial discretion, suggesting that impairments are clustered in periods in which other negative news is being absorbed and are systematically delayed in firms whose managers face contractual or career consequences from the loss recognition. @lisloan2017, in a paper titled "Has Goodwill Accounting Gone Bad?," argue that the impairment-only model has produced systematic overstatement of goodwill on U.S. balance sheets and that the cumulative magnitude of unrecognized goodwill impairments is substantial. The literature has not converged on a definitive verdict, but the broad pattern is that the 2001 change did, as the critics anticipated, give managers more discretion over the timing of loss recognition, and the use of that discretion has not always been benign.

::: {.callout-warning}
## A subtle implication

Goodwill is, in the framework of Chapter 5, a high-relevance, low-faithful-representation account. It is highly relevant because it captures a large fraction of the value of acquired businesses. It is low in faithful representation because the underlying components of goodwill — workforce value, synergies, going-concern value — are inherently difficult to verify. The post-2001 impairment-only model is a particular institutional choice in the trade-off, and the choice has both vindicators and critics.
:::

## The value-relevance debate and the empirical evidence on impairment

The 2001 special issue of the *Journal of Accounting and Economics* contained two papers that have framed the academic debate over the value relevance of accounting numbers. @holthausenwatts2001, in a paper titled "The Relevance of the Value-Relevance Literature for Financial Accounting Standard Setting," argue that the value-relevance literature has limited usefulness for standard-setting decisions. They identify several reasons. First, value-relevance studies typically demonstrate associations between accounting numbers and prices but do not establish that the accounting numbers cause the price reactions; the associations could reflect common dependence on underlying economic events that the accounting numbers and prices both reflect. Second, value-relevance studies frequently use aggregated measures that conflate the relevance of the accounting standard with the relevance of the underlying economic events being reported, making it difficult to extract guidance for specific standard-setting choices. Third, value-relevance studies are typically conducted on samples that pre-date the standard-setting decision being evaluated, raising concerns about external validity. Holthausen and Watts argue, in light of these concerns, that the standard setter should rely on a broader and more conceptual analysis rather than on value-relevance evidence alone.

@barthbeaverlandsman2001, in a paper published in the same issue, defend the value-relevance literature. They argue that the literature does provide useful evidence for standard setters, particularly when the empirical design isolates the effects of specific accounting choices and when the inference is appropriately qualified. Value-relevance evidence is one input into the standard-setting decision, on their view, not the sole basis for it. The Barth–Beaver–Landsman position has been more influential within the empirical-accounting subfield, while the Holthausen–Watts position has been more influential among standard setters and in policy-oriented discussions. The two papers do not principally disagree about the empirical evidence; they disagree about the inferences that can be drawn from it and about the role of empirical evidence in the standard-setting process.

The impairment literature speaks directly to this debate. Empirical work has documented that the timing of impairment recognition is, on average, partially driven by the underlying economics of the asset (i.e., impairments cluster in periods of adverse industry shocks and operating losses, consistent with the standard's stated purpose) but also partially driven by managerial discretion. @beattyweber2006 examine goodwill impairment decisions immediately following SFAS 142 and report that firms with debt covenants tied to net worth, firms with earnings-based bonus plans, and firms whose CEOs face career consequences from loss recognition are systematically slower to impair, consistent with the prediction that contracting incentives delay loss recognition. @lisloan2017 extend the analysis through more recent vintages and document that the cumulative undisclosed goodwill impairments under the post-2001 regime are economically meaningful at the aggregate level, suggesting that delayed recognition produces a stock of impaired goodwill that is not reflected on contemporaneous balance sheets.

The empirical pattern is consistent with both interpretations of the regime. The impairment standard does identify some impairments in a timely way (the standard works), and the impairment standard also leaves substantial room for managerial discretion that is, on average, used to delay loss recognition (the standard is incomplete). The evidence is suggestive but not definitive on the question of whether the post-2001 regime is superior to the pre-2001 amortization regime. The amortization model had its own problems — arbitrary allocations, no responsiveness to the underlying economics of individual goodwill balances — and the empirical record on a regime-versus-regime comparison is mixed. The literature has not produced a consensus recommendation for a return to amortization, but it has produced sustained scrutiny of the post-2001 model and continued attention to the topic in FASB deliberations.

## Mechanics: an extended Acme Corp. example

The Acme Corp. example continues. Recall that on January 2 of its first year of operations, Acme purchased a delivery truck for $50{,}000 financed with a $50{,}000 bank loan. We did not, at that point, depreciate the truck because the chapter had not yet introduced depreciation. We can now do so.

Suppose Acme estimates the truck's useful life at five years and its salvage value at $5{,}000. Under straight-line depreciation, the annual depreciation expense is
$$
\text{Annual depreciation} = \frac{\$50{,}000 - \$5{,}000}{5 \text{ years}} = \$9{,}000 \text{ per year}.
$$
Monthly depreciation is $750. The journal entry to record one month's depreciation is:

| Account                       | Debit | Credit |
|-------------------------------|-------|--------|
| Depreciation expense          | 750   |        |
| Accumulated depreciation      |       | 750    |

The expense reduces reported income; the accumulated depreciation account is a contra-asset that offsets the truck's gross balance on the balance sheet. After one year (twelve months), Acme's truck would appear as:

| Item                          | Amount |
|-------------------------------|--------|
| Truck, at cost                | $50,000 |
| Less: accumulated depreciation | (9,000) |
| Truck, net                    | $41,000 |

Suppose, three years into the truck's life, Acme replaces the truck's transmission for $3{,}000. The expenditure extends the truck's expected useful life by one year. Under U.S. GAAP, the expenditure is a *capital improvement* — it is added to the truck's cost basis, and the depreciation schedule is revised prospectively to reflect the new useful life and the new depreciable base.

Suppose, alternatively, that in year four Acme determines that the truck has become obsolete due to a shift in the firm's business and is no longer expected to generate cash flows sufficient to recover its remaining carrying amount. Acme would test the truck for impairment under ASC 360. Assume the carrying amount at the end of year four is $14{,}000 (cost of $50{,}000 less four years of straight-line depreciation at $9{,}000 per year). Suppose the truck's remaining undiscounted cash flows are estimated at $11{,}000 and its fair value (the present value of expected future cash flows) is estimated at $8{,}000. The recoverability test fails because the undiscounted cash flows ($11{,}000) are below the carrying amount ($14{,}000). Step two writes the truck down to fair value, producing a $6{,}000 impairment loss ($14{,}000 less $8{,}000). The new carrying amount becomes the basis for subsequent depreciation over the truck's remaining useful life.

The example is straightforward. It is included here because the underlying mechanics — capitalize at cost, depreciate over useful life, capitalize subsequent improvements, test for impairment when triggered — are the workhorse of long-term asset accounting under U.S. GAAP. Deeper applications (component depreciation under IFRS, complex leasing arrangements, asset retirement obligations) are available in any intermediate accounting textbook, and the SEC's staff accounting bulletins are the most authoritative source for narrow interpretive questions.

## Key takeaways

::: {.callout-important}
## Key takeaways

- **Historical cost is a stewardship choice, not an oversight.** U.S. GAAP carries most long-lived operating assets at historical cost (less accumulated depreciation) because verifiability and stewardship considerations outweigh the relevance gain from continuous fair-value remeasurement.
- **Depreciation allocates cost, not value.** Straight-line, double-declining-balance, and units-of-production methods produce identical lifetime depreciation on the same depreciable base, but they differ in the timing of expense recognition and therefore in the year-to-year pattern of reported earnings and carrying amount.
- **Impairment is a one-way ratchet.** Long-lived tangible assets are written down to fair value when undiscounted cash flows fall below carrying amount, but they are not written back up when the asset's economics improve — an asymmetry consistent with the conservatism principle of @watts2003a.
- **Intangibles are treated asymmetrically.** Acquired intangibles are recognized at cost or fair value and either amortized or tested for impairment annually under ASC 350; internally-developed intangibles are largely expensed as incurred under ASC 730, leaving a measurement gap that has grown with the intangibles-intensive economy.
- **Goodwill is the residual that captures what survives.** Goodwill arises only in business combinations as the excess of purchase price over the fair value of identifiable net assets, is not amortized post-SFAS 142, and is tested for impairment annually — a regime that gives managers discretion that the empirical literature finds is sometimes used to delay loss recognition.
- **The empirical record on impairment is mixed.** Impairments are sometimes timely and economically driven (e.g., Boeing's 737 MAX charges); impairments are sometimes delayed in ways consistent with managerial incentives (e.g., the empirical patterns in @beattyweber2006 and @lisloan2017).
:::
