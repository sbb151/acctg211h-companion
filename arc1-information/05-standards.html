5  Standard Setting and the Conceptual Framework

5.1 Opening question

The United States sets accounting standards in an unusual way. The Securities and Exchange Commission (i.e., the SEC, the federal agency created by the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 with plenary authority over the financial reporting of registrants) has, since 1973, delegated the day-to-day work of writing accounting standards to a private nonprofit body, the Financial Accounting Standards Board (i.e., the FASB). The SEC retains the formal power to override or supplement the FASB’s pronouncements, but has done so rarely. The result is a regulatory structure in which most of the substantive rule-making for U.S. public-company financial reporting is conducted at substantial remove from the federal government. Most other large economies set accounting standards through government bodies, or through international bodies in which government participation is more visible.

The structural choice raises a question this chapter takes up: why does the United States set accounting standards through a private body rather than through a public agency? Behind that question is a deeper one. Why do we have mandatory accounting standards at all? Chapter 2 argued that the lemons problem creates a demand for credible disclosure, and Chapter 3 argued that voluntary disclosure unravels only partially, leaving an incomplete equilibrium. Mandatory standards fill the gap, on one reading. But the standard-setting process is itself costly — both in the direct compliance burden on preparers (i.e., the firms that produce financial statements) and in the indirect cost of standards that turn out, in retrospect, to have been poorly designed. A serious treatment of standard setting takes both sides of this ledger.

This chapter develops the framework. It traces the institutional history (the SEC’s 1934 founding, the AICPA’s Committee on Accounting Procedure and Accounting Principles Board, the FASB’s creation in 1973, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 and the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board, and the FASB-IASB convergence project). It introduces the positive theory of accounting standards (Watts and Zimmerman 1978, which examines how firms respond to standards rather than what standards ought to be). It introduces the FASB Conceptual Framework (Financial Accounting Standards Board 2010, the FASB’s own articulation of the objective and qualitative characteristics of financial reporting). It draws on the empirical literature on the consequences of standard adoption, principally the natural experiment of mandatory adoption of International Financial Reporting Standards (Daske et al. 2008, who found that mandatory IFRS adoption was associated with increases in market liquidity and decreases in cost of capital; Christensen, Hail, and Leuz 2013, who showed that the effects were concentrated in countries that simultaneously strengthened enforcement). The chapter closes Arc I by stepping up from the firm-level analysis of Chapters 1 through 4 to the institutional level at which the rules of the reporting environment are set.

5.2 A short institutional history

U.S. accounting standard-setting evolved through three regimes. We sketch each briefly because each one is, in part, a response to the failure of the one before.

The pre-1934 era: stock-exchange listing rules. Before federal securities legislation, accounting requirements for publicly traded firms were set principally by the New York Stock Exchange through its listing agreements. The arrangement produced uneven coverage — firms not listed on the NYSE faced little disclosure obligation — and the substantive content of the listing rules was relatively thin. The 1929 stock market crash and the disclosures that emerged in the subsequent congressional hearings (the Pecora hearings of 1932–1934) generated political pressure for a federal regime.

The SEC and the AICPA, 1934–1973. The Securities Act of 1933 and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 created the SEC and gave it authority over the financial reporting of public companies. The Commission could have set accounting standards directly. It chose, instead, to delegate the substantive work to the accounting profession, retaining a supervisory role. The American Institute of Certified Public Accountants (i.e., the AICPA, the professional association of U.S. certified public accountants) took up the delegation through two successive bodies: the Committee on Accounting Procedure (CAP), which issued Accounting Research Bulletins from 1939 to 1959, and the Accounting Principles Board (APB), which issued Opinions from 1959 to 1973. Both bodies were part-time committees of practicing accountants. By the late 1960s the APB had become controversial: its members were widely viewed as too close to their audit clients, its pronouncements were criticized as ad hoc, and several specific opinions (notably the 1962–1964 investment tax credit episode, in which the APB reversed itself under industry pressure) suggested that the structure was not producing standards of stable quality.

The FASB era, 1973–present. In response, the AICPA convened the Wheat Committee, which recommended replacing the APB with an independent full-time body. The recommendation produced, in 1973, the present three-tier structure: the Financial Accounting Foundation (i.e., the FAF, a private nonprofit foundation that appoints board members and oversees funding), the FASB itself (a seven-member full-time board), and the Financial Accounting Standards Advisory Council (i.e., the FASAC, a constituent-advisory body). The SEC formally endorsed the FASB’s pronouncements as authoritative GAAP in Accounting Series Release No. 150 (1973), and has accepted them with rare exceptions ever since.

The FASB’s first three decades were not without controversy. The funding structure raised concerns about independence — until 2002, the FAF was funded principally by voluntary contributions from preparers and audit firms, which created at least the appearance of dependence on the parties whose reporting the FASB was regulating. The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (i.e., the federal legislation passed in the aftermath of the Enron, WorldCom, and Tyco failures) altered this in two ways relevant to this chapter. First, it created the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (i.e., the PCAOB, a federally chartered nonprofit that supervises the audits of public companies), shifting audit standard-setting away from the AICPA and toward a body with statutory authority. Second, it directed the SEC to fund the FASB through a mandatory accounting support fee assessed on public companies, removing the most direct lobbying channel between preparers and the standard setter.

Convergence with the IASB. The International Accounting Standards Board (i.e., the IASB, a private standard-setting body based in London that produces IFRS for adoption by jurisdictions outside the United States) and the FASB began a formal convergence project in 2002 with the Norwalk Agreement. The project produced several joint standards (revenue recognition, ASC 606 / IFRS 15, effective 2018; leases, ASC 842 / IFRS 16, effective 2019) but converged only partially in many areas and not at all in others (notably inventory, where U.S. GAAP continues to permit LIFO while IFRS does not). The SEC considered, but ultimately declined, a 2008–2012 proposal that would have allowed or required U.S. public companies to file under IFRS. The convergence project has since been described by both boards as substantially concluded, with the two bodies cooperating on specific projects but no longer pursuing a single global standard.

The institutional history is worth tracing because it locates the present structure in a particular set of compromises. We do not have the SEC setting standards directly; we have a private body operating under a public delegation. We do not have a single global standard; we have two large standards with substantial but incomplete overlap. Each feature of the structure is, in part, a response to a perceived failure of an earlier arrangement, and each carries forward both the strengths and the limitations of that response.

5.3 The economic theory of regulation

The standard economic model of regulation begins from a simple asymmetry. Regulation imposes concentrated costs on identifiable parties (i.e., the regulated firms, their employees, sometimes their customers) and produces diffuse benefits across a broader population (i.e., consumers, investors, society at large). The party bearing the concentrated cost has a strong incentive to lobby against the regulation; the parties bearing the diffuse benefit have a weak per-party incentive to lobby for it. The regulatory-capture literature (Stigler 1971, who modeled regulation as supplied to the industries with the highest demand for it; Easterbrook and Fischel 1984, who applied a similar logic to mandatory securities disclosure) takes seriously the possibility that, at equilibrium, regulation often serves the interests of the regulated parties more than the interests of those the regulation is nominally designed to protect.

Whether the regulatory-capture argument applies to accounting standard-setting is a contested empirical question. The argument predicts that the FASB’s pronouncements should, in equilibrium, reflect the preferences of preparers more than the preferences of users (i.e., investors, creditors, and other capital-market participants who consume financial statements). The pre-2002 funding structure described in Section 5.2 gave the prediction some bite. Since SOX, the FASB has been funded by mandatory accounting support fees, which removes the most direct lobbying channel but does not eliminate the underlying political economy. Constituents continue to participate intensively through the FASB’s due-process procedures: comment letters, public roundtables, and private meetings with the staff.

A more nuanced view, closer to the position the empirical literature has converged on, is that standard setting is influenced by all of the major constituencies — preparers, auditors, users, regulators, and academics — but that the influence is neither unidirectional nor reducible to capture. Different standards activate different coalitions. Standards that reduce reported earnings (e.g., FAS 123R on stock-based compensation, discussed in Section 5.4) face vigorous opposition from preparers but support from users; standards that primarily impose disclosure costs without altering recognized amounts face more uniform opposition from preparers. The equilibrium reflects a balance of these coalitions rather than a clean capture by any single one.

There is also a tension that runs through the structure of U.S. standard-setting itself. The FASB is, simultaneously, a private-sector self-regulator (drawing its members from the preparer, auditor, and user communities, operating without statutory authority over its own pronouncements) and an instrument of economic policy (issuing rules that bind every U.S. public company and that have measurable effects on capital markets). The two roles are not always compatible. Private-sector self-regulation prizes responsiveness to constituents and technical expertise; economic policy prizes accountability to the public interest and independence from regulated parties. Standard-setting institutions in other jurisdictions resolve the tension differently — the IASB, for example, is closer to the private-self-regulator pole and depends on jurisdictional adoption for legal force, while government-set standards in some economies are closer to the economic-policy pole. The U.S. structure occupies a middle position whose stability depends on continued SEC acceptance of FASB pronouncements and on the FAF’s ability to fund the FASB without recreating the appearance of preparer dependence.

Why this matters

The political-economy analysis is a tool for prediction, not just critique. Standards that generate large, concentrated compliance costs for major preparers face vigorous opposition during the comment-letter process. Standards that primarily benefit dispersed user constituencies struggle to generate the lobbying support needed to push them through. The framework gives us a way to think about why the FASB’s standards look the way they do.

5.4 The case for mandatory standards

A central economic question about accounting standards is why we have them at all. Why not let firms choose their own accounting policies, with the market disciplining inadequate disclosure through higher cost of capital? Three answers emerge from the literature.

Positive externalities of comparability. Investors pricing a firm need to compare it to peer firms in the same market. If each firm chooses its own measurement bases, the comparison requires the investor to translate across measurement conventions before drawing inferences. The translation is costly, and the cost is borne by each user separately, while the benefit of standardized measurement is enjoyed by all users collectively. A single firm cannot capture the full social benefit of conforming to a common measurement convention, because the benefit accrues to users who do not pay the firm for it. The argument is a textbook positive-externality case for coordination: each firm, choosing in isolation, may underinvest in comparability relative to what is socially optimal.

Coordination failures in voluntary disclosure. Chapter 3 developed the unraveling argument for voluntary disclosure, in which firms with favorable private information disclose to separate themselves from worse types. The argument is partial: unraveling stalls when disclosure is costly, when the relevant information is uncertain to the manager herself, or when the manager has multiple audiences with conflicting interests. Mandatory standards remove the manager’s discretion not to disclose, and so produce disclosure equilibria that voluntary regimes cannot reach. The argument does not depend on any particular standard being well-designed; it depends only on the claim that some baseline of disclosure is socially valuable and would not be supplied at sufficient volume by uncoordinated firms.

Network externalities and switching costs. Once a measurement convention is in place, the convention itself generates value that grows with the number of firms using it. Investors trained to read U.S. GAAP, auditors trained to attest to it, and software systems built to produce it constitute a network whose value to each participant rises with the size of the network. Network externalities argue for a single convention rather than multiple competing ones, and for stability of the convention over time. They are part of the reason that wholesale changes to GAAP — the IFRS adoption proposal of 2008–2012, for example — generate substantial resistance from preparers, auditors, and users alike. They are also part of the reason that the FASB-IASB convergence project ultimately stopped short of a single global standard: the costs of disrupting two large existing networks were judged to exceed the benefits of merging them.

Against these arguments stands a set of standard objections. Mandatory standards can codify particular measurement choices that turn out, in retrospect, to be inferior to alternatives the standards have foreclosed. The political-economy analysis of Section 5.3 suggests that the codified choices may reflect the influence of preparers more than that of users. And mandatory standards impose compliance costs that fall disproportionately on smaller firms, which face a fixed cost of standard-setting compliance that does not scale with firm size. The literature reflected in Easterbrook and Fischel (1984) takes the objections seriously and reaches a qualified conclusion: mandatory disclosure regulation is defensible when the benefits of standardization exceed the costs of regulatory error, but the comparison is sensitive to features of the specific standard and the institutional setting.

A concrete illustration: FAS 123R and stock-based compensation

The contested 2004 adoption of Statement of Financial Accounting Standards No. 123R (i.e., the standard requiring U.S. public companies to recognize the fair value of stock-based compensation as an expense on the income statement) illustrates the political economy and the externality argument together. Before FAS 123R, firms granting stock options to employees were permitted to disclose the fair value of the options in the footnotes while reporting zero compensation expense for the options on the income statement. The footnote disclosure was, in principle, sufficient to allow sophisticated users to compute an adjusted measure of earnings, but the disclosure was inconsistent across firms and the adjustment was costly to perform.

The FASB proposed mandatory recognition (i.e., bringing the expense onto the face of the income statement rather than leaving it in the footnotes) in the early 2000s. The proposal faced organized opposition from the technology industry, which argued that the resulting reduction in reported earnings would harm capital formation and that the fair value of employee stock options was difficult to estimate. Congress held hearings; legislation was introduced to override the FASB. The standard ultimately took effect in 2005–2006 in substantially the form the FASB had proposed. Subsequent empirical work documented that recognized stock-based compensation expense did enter the prices of affected firms more completely than the prior footnote disclosure had, consistent with the externality and unraveling arguments for mandatory recognition.

The episode is a clean illustration of the tension Section 5.3 described. Mandatory recognition of stock-based compensation was, in part, an exercise of economic policy — the FASB was choosing, on behalf of capital markets, how a particular form of compensation should be measured. It was also, in part, an exercise of private-sector self-regulation — the FASB’s decision survived congressional pressure because the SEC and a coalition of users supported it. The outcome was neither a clean capture by preparers nor a clean victory for users, but a contested equilibrium produced by the structure described in Section 5.2.

5.5 Watts and Zimmerman: a positive theory of standards

Watts and Zimmerman (1978) made a related but distinct contribution. Where the Stigler–Peltzman–Posner literature analyzed the supply of regulation, Watts and Zimmerman analyzed the demand for accounting choices — how firms respond to the discretion that accounting standards leave them. Their argument was framed as a positive theory (i.e., a theory about what firms actually do, rather than what they ought to do under some normative criterion). They asked which accounting methods firms select and what the regularities in those selections reveal about the economic incentives at work.

Watts and Zimmerman proposed three hypotheses, each of which generated a substantial empirical literature.

The first is the bonus plan hypothesis: managers whose compensation depends on reported earnings will, all else equal, select accounting methods that increase current-period earnings. The hypothesis follows from the moral hazard analysis of Chapter 4 — when the manager’s contract is contingent on a reported metric, the manager has an incentive to influence the metric in directions favorable to her compensation. Healy’s (1985) subsequent empirical work, discussed in Chapter 4, made the hypothesis concrete by documenting that managers respond to bonus-plan kinks by adjusting accruals in predictable directions.

The second is the debt/equity hypothesis: firms with higher leverage will, all else equal, select accounting methods that increase reported earnings or reported equity. The hypothesis follows from the contracting analysis we will develop in Chapter 9: debt contracts often condition on accounting numbers via covenants (i.e., contractual restrictions that limit what the firm may do if the numbers fall below specified thresholds). Firms close to a covenant boundary have an incentive to manage the relevant numbers, and accounting choices that increase the numbers are preferred.

The third is the political cost hypothesis: firms subject to political scrutiny will, all else equal, select accounting methods that decrease reported earnings. Politically visible firms (i.e., large firms, firms in regulated industries, and firms in consolidated industries that may attract antitrust attention) pay a cost when reported profits are unusually high, in the form of regulatory action, antitrust scrutiny, or unfavorable tax treatment. They have an incentive to mute reported earnings rather than amplify them.

The three hypotheses are positive rather than normative. Watts and Zimmerman did not claim the choices were optimal or socially desirable; they claimed only that the choices were predictable from the underlying economic incentives. The subsequent empirical literature has documented support for each hypothesis with varying strength, and the framework remains an organizing tool for the empirical literature on accounting choice.

The Watts-Zimmerman framework has implications for the standard-setting process itself. If firms respond to standards with the predictable patterns of the three hypotheses, then a standard setter who designs standards without anticipating those responses produces equilibria different from the ones intended. A standard that, in a frictionless world, would yield a particular distribution of reported earnings, will in practice produce a different distribution shaped by managerial discretion within the standard’s bounds. The implication is that “standards plus discretion” is the relevant unit of analysis, not “standards alone.”

5.6 The FASB Conceptual Framework

The FASB Conceptual Framework (i.e., the Statement of Financial Accounting Concepts, with the most recent integrated version published as Concepts Statement No. 8) is the FASB’s own articulation of the objective of financial reporting and the qualitative characteristics that financial information should possess. The Conceptual Framework is not itself authoritative GAAP — firms cannot violate the Conceptual Framework, and auditors cannot opine on conformity with it — but it serves as the conceptual scaffold the FASB uses when drafting and revising specific standards. It is also a useful introduction to the FASB’s own theory of what financial reporting is for.

The first chapter of the Conceptual Framework states the objective of general purpose financial reporting: to provide financial information about the reporting entity that is useful to existing and potential investors, lenders, and other creditors in making decisions about providing resources to the entity. The objective is explicitly decision-useful. The information is intended to support an external party’s decision to commit or withhold capital. It is not principally about managerial control, taxation, or regulatory compliance, though financial reports are used for those purposes as well.

The decision-usefulness orientation has consequences we will see throughout Arc II. It implies, for example, that information about future cash flows is often more relevant than information about historical cash inflows, even though the historical information is more verifiable. It implies that timely information is preferable to delayed information, even at some cost in precision. Each of these implications is contested in specific applications, and the trade-offs surface as the qualitative characteristics develop in the Framework’s third chapter.

Decision-usefulness vs. stewardship

A long-running debate concerns whether the objective of financial reporting should be decision-usefulness (the FASB position) or stewardship (the position more prominent in some European traditions and in the academic agency-theory literature of Chapter 4). The two objectives often produce similar conclusions about specific accounting choices but occasionally diverge. The 2018 update to the Conceptual Framework moved partway toward integrating stewardship, recognizing that information useful for monitoring management’s stewardship is one input into the decision to commit capital. The integration is uneasy, and the debate continues.

Qualitative characteristics: relevance and faithful representation. Chapter 3 of the Conceptual Framework articulates the qualitative characteristics that financial information should possess to satisfy the decision-usefulness objective. The Framework identifies two fundamental qualitative characteristics — relevance and faithful representation — and four enhancing qualitative characteristics — comparability, verifiability, timeliness, and understandability.

The fundamental characteristics are the two without which information is not useful for decision-making. Relevance means that the information has the capacity to make a difference in the decision being made: financial information is relevant if it has predictive value (i.e., it helps the user form expectations about future outcomes), confirmatory value (i.e., it provides feedback about prior expectations), or both. Faithful representation means that the information depicts the underlying economic phenomenon completely, neutrally, and free from material error.

The two fundamental characteristics frequently come into tension. Information about a long-lived intangible asset (e.g., a brand name developed over decades) is highly relevant to investors trying to value the firm, but it is difficult to measure faithfully because intangible asset valuation requires substantial estimation. Information about a financial instrument carried at amortized cost is faithfully represented (the carrying amount is grounded in observable historical transactions) but may be less relevant than the instrument’s current fair value. The Conceptual Framework treats this tension as inherent in the trade-off between the two characteristics and does not provide a single rule for resolving it. Each accounting standard navigates the trade-off in the context of its specific subject matter.

The four enhancing characteristics support the fundamental characteristics rather than substituting for them. Comparability requires that similar transactions be reported similarly across firms and across periods, so users can compare. Verifiability requires that knowledgeable, independent observers could reach consensus that the information represents the underlying phenomenon faithfully; verifiability is closely related to but distinct from faithful representation, in that verifiability concerns the capacity for independent verification rather than the absence of error itself. Timeliness requires that information be available in time to affect decisions, recognizing that delayed information is less useful even if more accurate. Understandability requires that information be presented clearly, with the intended user assumed to have a reasonable knowledge of business and economic activities.

Each of the enhancing characteristics is the framework’s response to a problem we have already encountered. Comparability is the response to the cross-firm-pooling problem implicit in the Akerlof model: investors pricing a firm need to compare it to other firms in the same market, and the comparison requires consistent measurement bases. Verifiability is the response to the credibility problem of Chapter 2: information that cannot in principle be verified by an independent third party loses much of its value as a credible report.

Common pitfall

The qualitative characteristics are sometimes presented in introductory courses as a hierarchy or a checklist. The Conceptual Framework does not endorse that view. The fundamental characteristics are necessary conditions; the enhancing characteristics raise or lower the value of information that already satisfies the fundamental characteristics. There is no formula by which a standard setter mechanically combines the characteristics to produce a standard. The trade-offs require judgment and are at the center of every contested standard-setting decision.

5.7 The standard-setting process in practice

The FASB’s standard-setting process is structured to balance technical expertise, accountability, and responsiveness to constituent input. The process is often referred to as the FASB’s due process. The principal elements are as follows.

The FASB itself consists of seven members appointed to renewable five-year terms. Members are typically drawn from the preparer, auditor, user, and academic communities, with substantial financial-reporting experience in each case. The chair is appointed by the trustees of the FAF. Day-to-day work is supported by a professional staff of approximately seventy.

A new project on the FASB’s technical agenda typically begins with a project proposal and the issuance of an invitation to comment or a discussion paper. Constituent feedback is solicited through written comment letters, public roundtables, and meetings with affected parties. The Board considers the feedback in formulating a tentative position, which is issued as an exposure draft of the proposed standard. Additional comment letters are solicited; the Board considers them and may revise the proposal. The final standard is issued as an Accounting Standards Update (i.e., an ASU, which amends the Accounting Standards Codification — the authoritative compilation of U.S. GAAP).

The process can take years. The lease accounting project that produced ASC 842 (i.e., the standard that brought operating leases onto the balance sheet) was on the FASB and IASB agenda for more than a decade before the final standard became effective in 2019. The crypto-asset project that produced ASU 2023-08 moved more quickly, but most major projects involve an extended period of consultation, drafting, and revision.

The Emerging Issues Task Force (i.e., the EITF, a standing committee of practitioners that addresses narrower interpretive questions arising in practice) handles questions that do not require a full FASB project. The EITF’s consensus positions are typically incorporated into the ASC.

The structure of the process gives substantial voice to constituent groups, particularly preparers and auditors who have the expertise and incentive to participate intensively. It is, in the framework of Section 5.3, a process designed to produce information-rich standard setting under conditions of significant constituent influence. Whether the resulting equilibrium reflects the public interest, the regulated parties’ interest, or some balance of the two is a question on which reasonable analysts disagree.

IFRS vs. GAAP: the LIFO divergence

A clean illustration of the limits of convergence is the treatment of inventory. U.S. GAAP permits firms to assign costs to inventory and cost of goods sold using the last-in-first-out method (i.e., LIFO, which assumes that the most recently purchased units are the first sold). IFRS prohibits LIFO and requires either first-in-first-out (i.e., FIFO, which assumes the earliest purchased units are sold first) or weighted-average cost. The divergence is consequential: in periods of rising input prices, LIFO produces a higher cost of goods sold and lower reported earnings than FIFO, with corresponding effects on reported inventory and on taxable income.

LIFO persists in U.S. GAAP for a reason that is institutional rather than conceptual. The U.S. Internal Revenue Code’s LIFO conformity rule requires that a firm using LIFO for tax purposes also use it for financial reporting. Eliminating LIFO from U.S. GAAP would, in effect, eliminate the tax deferral that LIFO provides to firms in industries with persistent input-price inflation. The political economy of that change has been sufficient to keep LIFO in U.S. GAAP through multiple convergence rounds. The episode illustrates that convergence is bounded not only by technical disagreement but by external institutional constraints (i.e., tax law, here) that operate outside the standard-setter’s control.

5.8 Empirical evidence on the consequences of standards

The empirical literature on the consequences of accounting standards is large and continues to grow. Two strands are particularly relevant.

The first strand examines the direct effects of specific standards on the financial statements of affected firms. The lease accounting change (i.e., ASC 842, effective 2019) added approximately two trillion dollars of operating-lease right-of-use assets and corresponding lease liabilities to the balance sheets of U.S. public companies. The revenue recognition change (i.e., ASC 606, effective 2018) reshaped the timing and amount of revenue reported by firms with multiple-element arrangements, particularly in software and telecommunications. The credit losses change (i.e., ASC 326, “CECL,” effective 2020 for public companies) altered the loss reserves of financial institutions in ways that were quantitatively material. Each of these standards generated a literature documenting its effects on reported numbers and, in some cases, on real corporate decisions. The empirical pattern is that standards do change reported numbers in the directions intended, although the magnitudes and spillovers onto real activity vary considerably.

The second strand examines the capital-market consequences of large-scale standard-setting changes. The most extensively studied case is the mandatory adoption of IFRS by approximately two dozen economies during 2005–2008. Daske et al. (2008) documented that mandatory IFRS adoption was associated, on average, with statistically significant increases in market liquidity and decreases in cost of capital. Christensen, Hail, and Leuz (2013) extended the analysis and showed that the effects were concentrated in countries that simultaneously strengthened enforcement institutions; in countries where IFRS adoption was layered onto weak enforcement, the effects were attenuated or absent. The interpretation is that the capital-market benefits of standardization depend not only on the standards themselves but on the institutional infrastructure that makes the standards credible.

The literature does not establish that any specific standard produced a net benefit to society after compliance costs are netted against capital-market gains. The cost-benefit calculation is difficult to perform and is contested in specific cases. The general claim is more modest: standards-based reporting environments produce capital-market benefits (i.e., lower cost of capital, narrower bid-ask spreads, higher liquidity) when paired with credible enforcement, and the benefits scale with the size of the affected economies’ capital markets. Whether the magnitude of the benefits exceeds the magnitude of the costs in any given case is, the literature suggests, sensitive to features of the specific standard and the institutional setting in which it operates.

5.9 Key takeaways

Key takeaways
  • U.S. accounting standard-setting is delegated. The SEC has formal authority over public-company reporting but, since 1973, has delegated substantive standard-setting to the FASB, a private nonprofit body overseen by the FAF.
  • The institutional structure is a product of historical compromise. The AICPA’s earlier CAP and APB were replaced because they were perceived as too close to audit clients; the FASB’s funding was restructured under SOX (2002) for similar reasons; convergence with IFRS produced a partial alignment but not a single global standard.
  • Mandatory standards address coordination failures voluntary disclosure cannot. Comparability is a positive externality, unraveling under voluntary disclosure is only partial, and network effects favor a single stable convention.
  • Standard-setting is simultaneously economic policy and private-sector self-regulation. The two roles are not always compatible, and the resulting equilibrium reflects influence from preparers, auditors, users, regulators, and academics rather than capture by any single constituency.
  • The Conceptual Framework grounds standards in decision-usefulness. Relevance and faithful representation are the fundamental qualitative characteristics, with comparability, verifiability, timeliness, and understandability as enhancing characteristics; trade-offs among them require judgment.
  • The empirical literature is supportive but conditional. Standardization produces capital-market benefits (lower cost of capital, higher liquidity) when paired with credible enforcement; the benefits are attenuated where enforcement is weak, suggesting that standards and institutions are complements rather than substitutes.

  2. Watts and Zimmerman’s three hypotheses (bonus plan, debt/equity, political cost) are positive hypotheses about firm behavior.   3. The Conceptual Framework treats relevance and faithful representation as fundamental qualitative characteristics that come into trade-off with each other. # Standard Setting and the Conceptual Framework {#sec-standards}

## Opening question

The United States sets accounting standards in an unusual way. The Securities and Exchange Commission (i.e., the SEC, the federal agency created by the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 with plenary authority over the financial reporting of registrants) has, since 1973, delegated the day-to-day work of writing accounting standards to a private nonprofit body, the Financial Accounting Standards Board (i.e., the FASB). The SEC retains the formal power to override or supplement the FASB's pronouncements, but has done so rarely. The result is a regulatory structure in which most of the substantive rule-making for U.S. public-company financial reporting is conducted at substantial remove from the federal government. Most other large economies set accounting standards through government bodies, or through international bodies in which government participation is more visible.

The structural choice raises a question this chapter takes up: why does the United States set accounting standards through a private body rather than through a public agency? Behind that question is a deeper one. Why do we have mandatory accounting standards at all? Chapter 2 argued that the lemons problem creates a demand for credible disclosure, and Chapter 3 argued that voluntary disclosure unravels only partially, leaving an incomplete equilibrium. Mandatory standards fill the gap, on one reading. But the standard-setting process is itself costly --- both in the direct compliance burden on preparers (i.e., the firms that produce financial statements) and in the indirect cost of standards that turn out, in retrospect, to have been poorly designed. A serious treatment of standard setting takes both sides of this ledger.

This chapter develops the framework. It traces the institutional history (the SEC's 1934 founding, the AICPA's Committee on Accounting Procedure and Accounting Principles Board, the FASB's creation in 1973, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 and the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board, and the FASB-IASB convergence project). It introduces the positive theory of accounting standards [@wattszimmerman1978, which examines how firms respond to standards rather than what standards ought to be]. It introduces the FASB Conceptual Framework [@fasb_cf, the FASB's own articulation of the objective and qualitative characteristics of financial reporting]. It draws on the empirical literature on the consequences of standard adoption, principally the natural experiment of mandatory adoption of International Financial Reporting Standards [@daskehailleuzverdi2008, who found that mandatory IFRS adoption was associated with increases in market liquidity and decreases in cost of capital; @christensenhailleuz2013, who showed that the effects were concentrated in countries that simultaneously strengthened enforcement]. The chapter closes Arc I by stepping up from the firm-level analysis of Chapters 1 through 4 to the institutional level at which the rules of the reporting environment are set.

## A short institutional history

U.S. accounting standard-setting evolved through three regimes. We sketch each briefly because each one is, in part, a response to the failure of the one before.

**The pre-1934 era: stock-exchange listing rules.** Before federal securities legislation, accounting requirements for publicly traded firms were set principally by the New York Stock Exchange through its listing agreements. The arrangement produced uneven coverage --- firms not listed on the NYSE faced little disclosure obligation --- and the substantive content of the listing rules was relatively thin. The 1929 stock market crash and the disclosures that emerged in the subsequent congressional hearings (the Pecora hearings of 1932--1934) generated political pressure for a federal regime.

**The SEC and the AICPA, 1934--1973.** The Securities Act of 1933 and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 created the SEC and gave it authority over the financial reporting of public companies. The Commission could have set accounting standards directly. It chose, instead, to delegate the substantive work to the accounting profession, retaining a supervisory role. The American Institute of Certified Public Accountants (i.e., the AICPA, the professional association of U.S. certified public accountants) took up the delegation through two successive bodies: the Committee on Accounting Procedure (CAP), which issued Accounting Research Bulletins from 1939 to 1959, and the Accounting Principles Board (APB), which issued Opinions from 1959 to 1973. Both bodies were part-time committees of practicing accountants. By the late 1960s the APB had become controversial: its members were widely viewed as too close to their audit clients, its pronouncements were criticized as ad hoc, and several specific opinions (notably the 1962--1964 investment tax credit episode, in which the APB reversed itself under industry pressure) suggested that the structure was not producing standards of stable quality.

**The FASB era, 1973--present.** In response, the AICPA convened the Wheat Committee, which recommended replacing the APB with an independent full-time body. The recommendation produced, in 1973, the present three-tier structure: the Financial Accounting Foundation (i.e., the FAF, a private nonprofit foundation that appoints board members and oversees funding), the FASB itself (a seven-member full-time board), and the Financial Accounting Standards Advisory Council (i.e., the FASAC, a constituent-advisory body). The SEC formally endorsed the FASB's pronouncements as authoritative GAAP in Accounting Series Release No. 150 (1973), and has accepted them with rare exceptions ever since.

The FASB's first three decades were not without controversy. The funding structure raised concerns about independence --- until 2002, the FAF was funded principally by voluntary contributions from preparers and audit firms, which created at least the appearance of dependence on the parties whose reporting the FASB was regulating. The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (i.e., the federal legislation passed in the aftermath of the Enron, WorldCom, and Tyco failures) altered this in two ways relevant to this chapter. First, it created the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (i.e., the PCAOB, a federally chartered nonprofit that supervises the audits of public companies), shifting audit standard-setting away from the AICPA and toward a body with statutory authority. Second, it directed the SEC to fund the FASB through a mandatory accounting support fee assessed on public companies, removing the most direct lobbying channel between preparers and the standard setter.

**Convergence with the IASB.** The International Accounting Standards Board (i.e., the IASB, a private standard-setting body based in London that produces IFRS for adoption by jurisdictions outside the United States) and the FASB began a formal convergence project in 2002 with the Norwalk Agreement. The project produced several joint standards (revenue recognition, ASC 606 / IFRS 15, effective 2018; leases, ASC 842 / IFRS 16, effective 2019) but converged only partially in many areas and not at all in others (notably inventory, where U.S. GAAP continues to permit LIFO while IFRS does not). The SEC considered, but ultimately declined, a 2008--2012 proposal that would have allowed or required U.S. public companies to file under IFRS. The convergence project has since been described by both boards as substantially concluded, with the two bodies cooperating on specific projects but no longer pursuing a single global standard.

The institutional history is worth tracing because it locates the present structure in a particular set of compromises. We do not have the SEC setting standards directly; we have a private body operating under a public delegation. We do not have a single global standard; we have two large standards with substantial but incomplete overlap. Each feature of the structure is, in part, a response to a perceived failure of an earlier arrangement, and each carries forward both the strengths and the limitations of that response.

## The economic theory of regulation

The standard economic model of regulation begins from a simple asymmetry. Regulation imposes concentrated costs on identifiable parties (i.e., the regulated firms, their employees, sometimes their customers) and produces diffuse benefits across a broader population (i.e., consumers, investors, society at large). The party bearing the concentrated cost has a strong incentive to lobby against the regulation; the parties bearing the diffuse benefit have a weak per-party incentive to lobby for it. The regulatory-capture literature [@stigler1971, who modeled regulation as supplied to the industries with the highest demand for it; @easterbrookfischel1984, who applied a similar logic to mandatory securities disclosure] takes seriously the possibility that, at equilibrium, regulation often serves the interests of the regulated parties more than the interests of those the regulation is nominally designed to protect.

Whether the regulatory-capture argument applies to accounting standard-setting is a contested empirical question. The argument predicts that the FASB's pronouncements should, in equilibrium, reflect the preferences of preparers more than the preferences of users (i.e., investors, creditors, and other capital-market participants who consume financial statements). The pre-2002 funding structure described in Section 5.2 gave the prediction some bite. Since SOX, the FASB has been funded by mandatory accounting support fees, which removes the most direct lobbying channel but does not eliminate the underlying political economy. Constituents continue to participate intensively through the FASB's due-process procedures: comment letters, public roundtables, and private meetings with the staff.

A more nuanced view, closer to the position the empirical literature has converged on, is that standard setting is influenced by all of the major constituencies --- preparers, auditors, users, regulators, and academics --- but that the influence is neither unidirectional nor reducible to capture. Different standards activate different coalitions. Standards that reduce reported earnings (e.g., FAS 123R on stock-based compensation, discussed in Section 5.4) face vigorous opposition from preparers but support from users; standards that primarily impose disclosure costs without altering recognized amounts face more uniform opposition from preparers. The equilibrium reflects a balance of these coalitions rather than a clean capture by any single one.

There is also a tension that runs through the structure of U.S. standard-setting itself. The FASB is, simultaneously, a private-sector self-regulator (drawing its members from the preparer, auditor, and user communities, operating without statutory authority over its own pronouncements) and an instrument of economic policy (issuing rules that bind every U.S. public company and that have measurable effects on capital markets). The two roles are not always compatible. Private-sector self-regulation prizes responsiveness to constituents and technical expertise; economic policy prizes accountability to the public interest and independence from regulated parties. Standard-setting institutions in other jurisdictions resolve the tension differently --- the IASB, for example, is closer to the private-self-regulator pole and depends on jurisdictional adoption for legal force, while government-set standards in some economies are closer to the economic-policy pole. The U.S. structure occupies a middle position whose stability depends on continued SEC acceptance of FASB pronouncements and on the FAF's ability to fund the FASB without recreating the appearance of preparer dependence.

::: {.callout-note}
## Why this matters

The political-economy analysis is a tool for prediction, not just critique. Standards that generate large, concentrated compliance costs for major preparers face vigorous opposition during the comment-letter process. Standards that primarily benefit dispersed user constituencies struggle to generate the lobbying support needed to push them through. The framework gives us a way to think about why the FASB's standards look the way they do.
:::

## The case for mandatory standards

A central economic question about accounting standards is why we have them at all. Why not let firms choose their own accounting policies, with the market disciplining inadequate disclosure through higher cost of capital? Three answers emerge from the literature.

**Positive externalities of comparability.** Investors pricing a firm need to compare it to peer firms in the same market. If each firm chooses its own measurement bases, the comparison requires the investor to translate across measurement conventions before drawing inferences. The translation is costly, and the cost is borne by each user separately, while the benefit of standardized measurement is enjoyed by all users collectively. A single firm cannot capture the full social benefit of conforming to a common measurement convention, because the benefit accrues to users who do not pay the firm for it. The argument is a textbook positive-externality case for coordination: each firm, choosing in isolation, may underinvest in comparability relative to what is socially optimal.

**Coordination failures in voluntary disclosure.** Chapter 3 developed the unraveling argument for voluntary disclosure, in which firms with favorable private information disclose to separate themselves from worse types. The argument is partial: unraveling stalls when disclosure is costly, when the relevant information is uncertain to the manager herself, or when the manager has multiple audiences with conflicting interests. Mandatory standards remove the manager's discretion not to disclose, and so produce disclosure equilibria that voluntary regimes cannot reach. The argument does not depend on any particular standard being well-designed; it depends only on the claim that some baseline of disclosure is socially valuable and would not be supplied at sufficient volume by uncoordinated firms.

**Network externalities and switching costs.** Once a measurement convention is in place, the convention itself generates value that grows with the number of firms using it. Investors trained to read U.S. GAAP, auditors trained to attest to it, and software systems built to produce it constitute a network whose value to each participant rises with the size of the network. Network externalities argue for a single convention rather than multiple competing ones, and for stability of the convention over time. They are part of the reason that wholesale changes to GAAP --- the IFRS adoption proposal of 2008--2012, for example --- generate substantial resistance from preparers, auditors, and users alike. They are also part of the reason that the FASB-IASB convergence project ultimately stopped short of a single global standard: the costs of disrupting two large existing networks were judged to exceed the benefits of merging them.

Against these arguments stands a set of standard objections. Mandatory standards can codify particular measurement choices that turn out, in retrospect, to be inferior to alternatives the standards have foreclosed. The political-economy analysis of Section 5.3 suggests that the codified choices may reflect the influence of preparers more than that of users. And mandatory standards impose compliance costs that fall disproportionately on smaller firms, which face a fixed cost of standard-setting compliance that does not scale with firm size. The literature reflected in @easterbrookfischel1984 takes the objections seriously and reaches a qualified conclusion: mandatory disclosure regulation is defensible when the benefits of standardization exceed the costs of regulatory error, but the comparison is sensitive to features of the specific standard and the institutional setting.

::: {.callout-note}
## A concrete illustration: FAS 123R and stock-based compensation

The contested 2004 adoption of Statement of Financial Accounting Standards No. 123R (i.e., the standard requiring U.S. public companies to recognize the fair value of stock-based compensation as an expense on the income statement) illustrates the political economy and the externality argument together. Before FAS 123R, firms granting stock options to employees were permitted to disclose the fair value of the options in the footnotes while reporting zero compensation expense for the options on the income statement. The footnote disclosure was, in principle, sufficient to allow sophisticated users to compute an adjusted measure of earnings, but the disclosure was inconsistent across firms and the adjustment was costly to perform.

The FASB proposed mandatory recognition (i.e., bringing the expense onto the face of the income statement rather than leaving it in the footnotes) in the early 2000s. The proposal faced organized opposition from the technology industry, which argued that the resulting reduction in reported earnings would harm capital formation and that the fair value of employee stock options was difficult to estimate. Congress held hearings; legislation was introduced to override the FASB. The standard ultimately took effect in 2005--2006 in substantially the form the FASB had proposed. Subsequent empirical work documented that recognized stock-based compensation expense did enter the prices of affected firms more completely than the prior footnote disclosure had, consistent with the externality and unraveling arguments for mandatory recognition.

The episode is a clean illustration of the tension Section 5.3 described. Mandatory recognition of stock-based compensation was, in part, an exercise of economic policy --- the FASB was choosing, on behalf of capital markets, how a particular form of compensation should be measured. It was also, in part, an exercise of private-sector self-regulation --- the FASB's decision survived congressional pressure because the SEC and a coalition of users supported it. The outcome was neither a clean capture by preparers nor a clean victory for users, but a contested equilibrium produced by the structure described in Section 5.2.
:::

## Watts and Zimmerman: a positive theory of standards

@wattszimmerman1978 made a related but distinct contribution. Where the Stigler--Peltzman--Posner literature analyzed the *supply* of regulation, Watts and Zimmerman analyzed the *demand* for accounting choices --- how firms respond to the discretion that accounting standards leave them. Their argument was framed as a positive theory (i.e., a theory about what firms actually do, rather than what they ought to do under some normative criterion). They asked which accounting methods firms select and what the regularities in those selections reveal about the economic incentives at work.

Watts and Zimmerman proposed three hypotheses, each of which generated a substantial empirical literature.

The first is the *bonus plan hypothesis*: managers whose compensation depends on reported earnings will, all else equal, select accounting methods that increase current-period earnings. The hypothesis follows from the moral hazard analysis of Chapter 4 --- when the manager's contract is contingent on a reported metric, the manager has an incentive to influence the metric in directions favorable to her compensation. Healy's [-@healy1985] subsequent empirical work, discussed in Chapter 4, made the hypothesis concrete by documenting that managers respond to bonus-plan kinks by adjusting accruals in predictable directions.

The second is the *debt/equity hypothesis*: firms with higher leverage will, all else equal, select accounting methods that increase reported earnings or reported equity. The hypothesis follows from the contracting analysis we will develop in Chapter 9: debt contracts often condition on accounting numbers via covenants (i.e., contractual restrictions that limit what the firm may do if the numbers fall below specified thresholds). Firms close to a covenant boundary have an incentive to manage the relevant numbers, and accounting choices that increase the numbers are preferred.

The third is the *political cost hypothesis*: firms subject to political scrutiny will, all else equal, select accounting methods that decrease reported earnings. Politically visible firms (i.e., large firms, firms in regulated industries, and firms in consolidated industries that may attract antitrust attention) pay a cost when reported profits are unusually high, in the form of regulatory action, antitrust scrutiny, or unfavorable tax treatment. They have an incentive to mute reported earnings rather than amplify them.

The three hypotheses are positive rather than normative. Watts and Zimmerman did not claim the choices were optimal or socially desirable; they claimed only that the choices were predictable from the underlying economic incentives. The subsequent empirical literature has documented support for each hypothesis with varying strength, and the framework remains an organizing tool for the empirical literature on accounting choice.

The Watts-Zimmerman framework has implications for the standard-setting process itself. If firms respond to standards with the predictable patterns of the three hypotheses, then a standard setter who designs standards without anticipating those responses produces equilibria different from the ones intended. A standard that, in a frictionless world, would yield a particular distribution of reported earnings, will in practice produce a different distribution shaped by managerial discretion within the standard's bounds. The implication is that "standards plus discretion" is the relevant unit of analysis, not "standards alone."

## The FASB Conceptual Framework

The FASB Conceptual Framework (i.e., the *Statement of Financial Accounting Concepts*, with the most recent integrated version published as Concepts Statement No. 8) is the FASB's own articulation of the objective of financial reporting and the qualitative characteristics that financial information should possess. The Conceptual Framework is not itself authoritative GAAP --- firms cannot violate the Conceptual Framework, and auditors cannot opine on conformity with it --- but it serves as the conceptual scaffold the FASB uses when drafting and revising specific standards. It is also a useful introduction to the FASB's own theory of what financial reporting is for.

The first chapter of the Conceptual Framework states the *objective of general purpose financial reporting*: to provide financial information about the reporting entity that is useful to existing and potential investors, lenders, and other creditors in making decisions about providing resources to the entity. The objective is explicitly *decision-useful*. The information is intended to support an external party's decision to commit or withhold capital. It is not principally about managerial control, taxation, or regulatory compliance, though financial reports are used for those purposes as well.

The decision-usefulness orientation has consequences we will see throughout Arc II. It implies, for example, that information about future cash flows is often more relevant than information about historical cash inflows, even though the historical information is more verifiable. It implies that timely information is preferable to delayed information, even at some cost in precision. Each of these implications is contested in specific applications, and the trade-offs surface as the qualitative characteristics develop in the Framework's third chapter.

::: {.callout-note}
## Decision-usefulness vs. stewardship

A long-running debate concerns whether the objective of financial reporting should be decision-usefulness (the FASB position) or stewardship (the position more prominent in some European traditions and in the academic agency-theory literature of Chapter 4). The two objectives often produce similar conclusions about specific accounting choices but occasionally diverge. The 2018 update to the Conceptual Framework moved partway toward integrating stewardship, recognizing that information useful for monitoring management's stewardship is one input into the decision to commit capital. The integration is uneasy, and the debate continues.
:::

**Qualitative characteristics: relevance and faithful representation.** Chapter 3 of the Conceptual Framework articulates the qualitative characteristics that financial information should possess to satisfy the decision-usefulness objective. The Framework identifies two *fundamental* qualitative characteristics --- relevance and faithful representation --- and four *enhancing* qualitative characteristics --- comparability, verifiability, timeliness, and understandability.

The fundamental characteristics are the two without which information is not useful for decision-making. *Relevance* means that the information has the capacity to make a difference in the decision being made: financial information is relevant if it has predictive value (i.e., it helps the user form expectations about future outcomes), confirmatory value (i.e., it provides feedback about prior expectations), or both. *Faithful representation* means that the information depicts the underlying economic phenomenon completely, neutrally, and free from material error.

The two fundamental characteristics frequently come into tension. Information about a long-lived intangible asset (e.g., a brand name developed over decades) is highly relevant to investors trying to value the firm, but it is difficult to measure faithfully because intangible asset valuation requires substantial estimation. Information about a financial instrument carried at amortized cost is faithfully represented (the carrying amount is grounded in observable historical transactions) but may be less relevant than the instrument's current fair value. The Conceptual Framework treats this tension as inherent in the trade-off between the two characteristics and does not provide a single rule for resolving it. Each accounting standard navigates the trade-off in the context of its specific subject matter.

The four enhancing characteristics support the fundamental characteristics rather than substituting for them. *Comparability* requires that similar transactions be reported similarly across firms and across periods, so users can compare. *Verifiability* requires that knowledgeable, independent observers could reach consensus that the information represents the underlying phenomenon faithfully; verifiability is closely related to but distinct from faithful representation, in that verifiability concerns the *capacity* for independent verification rather than the absence of error itself. *Timeliness* requires that information be available in time to affect decisions, recognizing that delayed information is less useful even if more accurate. *Understandability* requires that information be presented clearly, with the intended user assumed to have a reasonable knowledge of business and economic activities.

Each of the enhancing characteristics is the framework's response to a problem we have already encountered. Comparability is the response to the cross-firm-pooling problem implicit in the Akerlof model: investors pricing a firm need to compare it to other firms in the same market, and the comparison requires consistent measurement bases. Verifiability is the response to the credibility problem of Chapter 2: information that cannot in principle be verified by an independent third party loses much of its value as a credible report.

::: {.callout-warning}
## Common pitfall

The qualitative characteristics are sometimes presented in introductory courses as a hierarchy or a checklist. The Conceptual Framework does not endorse that view. The fundamental characteristics are necessary conditions; the enhancing characteristics raise or lower the value of information that already satisfies the fundamental characteristics. There is no formula by which a standard setter mechanically combines the characteristics to produce a standard. The trade-offs require judgment and are at the center of every contested standard-setting decision.
:::

## The standard-setting process in practice

The FASB's standard-setting process is structured to balance technical expertise, accountability, and responsiveness to constituent input. The process is often referred to as the FASB's *due process*. The principal elements are as follows.

The FASB itself consists of seven members appointed to renewable five-year terms. Members are typically drawn from the preparer, auditor, user, and academic communities, with substantial financial-reporting experience in each case. The chair is appointed by the trustees of the FAF. Day-to-day work is supported by a professional staff of approximately seventy.

A new project on the FASB's technical agenda typically begins with a *project proposal* and the issuance of an *invitation to comment* or a *discussion paper*. Constituent feedback is solicited through written comment letters, public roundtables, and meetings with affected parties. The Board considers the feedback in formulating a tentative position, which is issued as an *exposure draft* of the proposed standard. Additional comment letters are solicited; the Board considers them and may revise the proposal. The final standard is issued as an *Accounting Standards Update* (i.e., an ASU, which amends the Accounting Standards Codification --- the authoritative compilation of U.S. GAAP).

The process can take years. The lease accounting project that produced ASC 842 (i.e., the standard that brought operating leases onto the balance sheet) was on the FASB and IASB agenda for more than a decade before the final standard became effective in 2019. The crypto-asset project that produced ASU 2023-08 moved more quickly, but most major projects involve an extended period of consultation, drafting, and revision.

The Emerging Issues Task Force (i.e., the EITF, a standing committee of practitioners that addresses narrower interpretive questions arising in practice) handles questions that do not require a full FASB project. The EITF's consensus positions are typically incorporated into the ASC.

The structure of the process gives substantial voice to constituent groups, particularly preparers and auditors who have the expertise and incentive to participate intensively. It is, in the framework of Section 5.3, a process designed to produce information-rich standard setting under conditions of significant constituent influence. Whether the resulting equilibrium reflects the public interest, the regulated parties' interest, or some balance of the two is a question on which reasonable analysts disagree.

::: {.callout-note}
## IFRS vs. GAAP: the LIFO divergence

A clean illustration of the limits of convergence is the treatment of inventory. U.S. GAAP permits firms to assign costs to inventory and cost of goods sold using the last-in-first-out method (i.e., LIFO, which assumes that the most recently purchased units are the first sold). IFRS prohibits LIFO and requires either first-in-first-out (i.e., FIFO, which assumes the earliest purchased units are sold first) or weighted-average cost. The divergence is consequential: in periods of rising input prices, LIFO produces a higher cost of goods sold and lower reported earnings than FIFO, with corresponding effects on reported inventory and on taxable income.

LIFO persists in U.S. GAAP for a reason that is institutional rather than conceptual. The U.S. Internal Revenue Code's LIFO conformity rule requires that a firm using LIFO for tax purposes also use it for financial reporting. Eliminating LIFO from U.S. GAAP would, in effect, eliminate the tax deferral that LIFO provides to firms in industries with persistent input-price inflation. The political economy of that change has been sufficient to keep LIFO in U.S. GAAP through multiple convergence rounds. The episode illustrates that convergence is bounded not only by technical disagreement but by external institutional constraints (i.e., tax law, here) that operate outside the standard-setter's control.
:::

## Empirical evidence on the consequences of standards

The empirical literature on the consequences of accounting standards is large and continues to grow. Two strands are particularly relevant.

The first strand examines the *direct effects* of specific standards on the financial statements of affected firms. The lease accounting change (i.e., ASC 842, effective 2019) added approximately two trillion dollars of operating-lease right-of-use assets and corresponding lease liabilities to the balance sheets of U.S. public companies. The revenue recognition change (i.e., ASC 606, effective 2018) reshaped the timing and amount of revenue reported by firms with multiple-element arrangements, particularly in software and telecommunications. The credit losses change (i.e., ASC 326, "CECL," effective 2020 for public companies) altered the loss reserves of financial institutions in ways that were quantitatively material. Each of these standards generated a literature documenting its effects on reported numbers and, in some cases, on real corporate decisions. The empirical pattern is that standards do change reported numbers in the directions intended, although the magnitudes and spillovers onto real activity vary considerably.

The second strand examines the *capital-market consequences* of large-scale standard-setting changes. The most extensively studied case is the mandatory adoption of IFRS by approximately two dozen economies during 2005--2008. @daskehailleuzverdi2008 documented that mandatory IFRS adoption was associated, on average, with statistically significant increases in market liquidity and decreases in cost of capital. @christensenhailleuz2013 extended the analysis and showed that the effects were concentrated in countries that simultaneously strengthened enforcement institutions; in countries where IFRS adoption was layered onto weak enforcement, the effects were attenuated or absent. The interpretation is that the capital-market benefits of standardization depend not only on the standards themselves but on the institutional infrastructure that makes the standards credible.

The literature does not establish that any specific standard produced a net benefit to society after compliance costs are netted against capital-market gains. The cost-benefit calculation is difficult to perform and is contested in specific cases. The general claim is more modest: standards-based reporting environments produce capital-market benefits (i.e., lower cost of capital, narrower bid-ask spreads, higher liquidity) when paired with credible enforcement, and the benefits scale with the size of the affected economies' capital markets. Whether the magnitude of the benefits exceeds the magnitude of the costs in any given case is, the literature suggests, sensitive to features of the specific standard and the institutional setting in which it operates.

## Key takeaways

::: {.callout-important}
## Key takeaways

- **U.S. accounting standard-setting is delegated.** The SEC has formal authority over public-company reporting but, since 1973, has delegated substantive standard-setting to the FASB, a private nonprofit body overseen by the FAF.
- **The institutional structure is a product of historical compromise.** The AICPA's earlier CAP and APB were replaced because they were perceived as too close to audit clients; the FASB's funding was restructured under SOX (2002) for similar reasons; convergence with IFRS produced a partial alignment but not a single global standard.
- **Mandatory standards address coordination failures voluntary disclosure cannot.** Comparability is a positive externality, unraveling under voluntary disclosure is only partial, and network effects favor a single stable convention.
- **Standard-setting is simultaneously economic policy and private-sector self-regulation.** The two roles are not always compatible, and the resulting equilibrium reflects influence from preparers, auditors, users, regulators, and academics rather than capture by any single constituency.
- **The Conceptual Framework grounds standards in decision-usefulness.** Relevance and faithful representation are the fundamental qualitative characteristics, with comparability, verifiability, timeliness, and understandability as enhancing characteristics; trade-offs among them require judgment.
- **The empirical literature is supportive but conditional.** Standardization produces capital-market benefits (lower cost of capital, higher liquidity) when paired with credible enforcement; the benefits are attenuated where enforcement is weak, suggesting that standards and institutions are complements rather than substitutes.
:::
